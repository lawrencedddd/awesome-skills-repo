# Chapter 4: Tax-Sharing Reform and Its Effects

## Core Idea
The 1994 tax-sharing reform recentralized revenue without equivalently recentralizing expenditure, transforming local fiscal pressure and behavior.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
After VAT became mainly central revenue, industrial expansion yielded less fiscal return to local governments than before. Local incentives shifted toward business tax, construction, real estate, fees, and land-related revenue.

## Key Takeaways
1. 分税制 was both standardizing and centralizing.
2. Local fiscal pressure was not accidental; it was built into the new assignment of revenue and responsibility.
3. The later rise of land finance is tied to this squeeze.

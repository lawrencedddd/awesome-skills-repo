# Chapter 1: Introduction: Government Behavior and Central-Local Relations

## Core Idea
The book treats fiscal relations as a relational mechanism that shapes how governments act, not merely as budget rules.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fiscal sociology lens**: Analyze fiscal institutions by asking how money relations connect central government, local governments, firms, peasants, and public-service providers.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
When a locality pursues GDP growth, the action cannot be explained only by market opportunity or an official's personality. It sits at the intersection of fiscal revenue incentives, promotion pressure, and local control over land, firms, and administrative resources.

## Key Takeaways
1. Fiscal rules become behavior through intergovernmental relations.
2. China's decentralization is better understood as controlled delegation than as federalism.
3. Quantifiable targets make government efficient, but can make it efficient in the wrong direction.

# Chapter 11: Grassroots Finance in Developed Regions

## Core Idea
Developed regions reveal a different grassroots problem: more revenue can create stronger local capacity, but also stronger incentives for construction-led fiscal expansion.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A coastal township or county with strong industry and land value can fund visible construction and services while also deepening reliance on real-estate and construction-related fiscal sources.

## Key Takeaways
1. Fiscal pressure and fiscal abundance can both distort behavior.
2. Developed areas show how local autonomy reappears through land and construction.
3. Regional differences are produced by fiscal structure, not only market development.

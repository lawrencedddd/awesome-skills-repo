# Chapter 6: County-Township Fiscal Crisis

## Core Idea
The county-township fiscal crisis arose when grassroots governments retained personnel and service burdens while losing stable, discretionary revenue.

## Frameworks Introduced

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
Reducing peasant fees can improve state-peasant relations, but if replacement transfers cover only payroll, township governments may survive organizationally while losing capacity to provide rural services.

## Key Takeaways
1. Peasant burden was a fiscal-relations problem, not only a rural governance problem.
2. Tax-fee reform changed central-local relations by shifting costs upward.
3. A government can remain staffed while losing practical governing capacity.

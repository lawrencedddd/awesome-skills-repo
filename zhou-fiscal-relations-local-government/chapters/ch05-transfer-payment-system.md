# Chapter 5: The Establishment of the Transfer Payment System

## Core Idea
Transfer payments reconnected centralized revenue to local spending, but they also changed local governments' orientation from local extraction to upward fund-seeking.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A county may receive substantial funds, but if most arrive as earmarked transfers, officials cannot freely use them for wages, maintenance, or locally urgent tasks.

## Key Takeaways
1. Transfers solve one fiscal gap while creating new dependency.
2. Earmarked funds increase formal control but can reduce local adaptability.
3. The allocation process becomes a political and administrative arena.

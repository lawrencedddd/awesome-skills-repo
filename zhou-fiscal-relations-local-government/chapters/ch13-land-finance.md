# Chapter 13: Land Fiscalism

## Core Idea
Land finance created a second fiscal system centered on land revenue, while also feeding the formal budget through construction and real-estate taxes.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **First finance / second finance**: Distinguish formal budget revenue from land-centered fund revenue controlled by local governments.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A city can use land revenue for infrastructure, infrastructure raises land values, construction activity generates local taxes, and those taxes strengthen the fiscal case for more development.

## Key Takeaways
1. Land finance is not separate from budget finance; it reinforces it.
2. Formal fiscal centralization can push local revenue-seeking into informal or fund-based channels.
3. A city can become fiscally dependent on construction momentum.

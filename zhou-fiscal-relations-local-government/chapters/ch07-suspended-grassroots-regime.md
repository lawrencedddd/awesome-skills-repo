# Chapter 7: The Suspended State of Grassroots Regimes

## Core Idea
After tax-fee reform, some township governments became 'suspended': no longer extracting from peasants, but also less connected to rural public-service provision.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A township that once collected fees and organized rural works may, after reform, depend on county transfers for payroll while county departments decide education, water, roads, and other services.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ending extraction can weaken local connection if service capacity is not rebuilt.
2. Suspension is a relational condition between state and peasants.
3. County-centered finance can make village needs less visible.

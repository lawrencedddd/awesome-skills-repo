# Chapter 9: Rural Basic Public Services

Rural public-service provision depends not only on total fiscal resources but on whether resources reach the institutions and people who know local needs.

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A county may receive money for rural services, but if funds stay in county-level construction and bureau programs, villages experience visible county prosperity alongside rural decline.

## Key Takeaways
1. Equalization requires delivery mechanisms, not only transfer formulas.
2. Public services fail when finance bypasses local knowledge.
3. Rural service gaps reveal the limits of fiscal formalization.

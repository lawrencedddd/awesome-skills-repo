# Chapter 3: Effects and Consequences of Fiscal Contracting

## Core Idea
Fiscal contracting made local governments residual claimants over growth, pushing them to cultivate enterprises and bargain upward.

## Frameworks Introduced

## Mental Models
- Think of fiscal institutions as **relations among actors**, not just rules on paper.
- Use **money flow as behavior flow**: where funds come from changes where officials look, bargain, and report.
- Separate **formal control** from **substantive control**: tighter documents can coexist with more creative local workaround behavior.

## Anti-patterns
- **Government as one actor**: misses conflicts between center, province, county, township, and bureaus.
- **Policy intention reading**: assumes the stated purpose of a reform is the same as its behavioral consequence.
- **Revenue-only analysis**: ignores expenditure responsibility, personnel control, land authority, and project channels.

## Worked Example
A county government helps a township enterprise obtain credit not simply because it loves firms, but because enterprise growth expands the local fiscal base and strengthens the county's position in future fiscal bargaining.

## Key Takeaways
1. Fiscal contracting explains why local governments supported township enterprises.
2. The system raised local incentives but weakened central fiscal extraction.
3. Local government company-like behavior begins before land finance.

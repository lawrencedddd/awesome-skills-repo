# Fiscal Relations Cheatsheet

## Fast Rules

| Situation | Use This Rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Local behavior seems irrational | Map fiscal relations first | Behavior often follows revenue and responsibility assignments |
| Revenue is centralized | Look for local substitute revenue | Squeezed discretion moves into fees, land, funds, or platforms |
| Transfers increase | Ask whether they are general or earmarked | Earmarking changes control, discretion, and information flow |
| A township looks inactive | Test for suspension, not laziness | It may lack money, tasks, and service channels |
| Public service money exists but service is weak | Map the service chain | Funds may stop at county departments or projects |
| Land sales boom | Analyze both first finance and second finance | Land revenue also expands construction-related taxes |
| Urban construction accelerates | Check the trinity loop | Land collateral and bank credit may be driving expansion |
| Policy documents look clean | Inspect implementation relations | Formal rules travel through departments, people, and bargaining |
| Performance is quantified | Watch for measurability trap | Easy-to-count outputs can displace welfare and fairness |

## Mechanism Maps

**Fiscal contracting**: local marginal revenue retention -> enterprise nurturing -> tax-base growth -> bargaining power -> central revenue pressure.

**Tax-sharing reform**: central revenue share rises -> local discretionary revenue falls -> transfers expand -> earmarking grows -> local governments seek controllable revenue.

**Suspended grassroots regime**: tax-fee reform -> township extraction declines -> county/bureau fiscal channels rise -> township loses discretion -> rural service connection weakens.

**Land-fiscal-financial trinity**: land control -> land reserve/platform -> collateralized bank credit -> infrastructure and expropriation -> land conveyance and construction taxes -> more borrowing.

## Questions To Ask

1. Which government level gains revenue from the action?
2. Which level bears expenditure responsibility?
3. Is the money discretionary, earmarked, budgetary, fund-based, or debt-financed?
4. Who has the best local information?
5. Who controls approval and inspection?
6. What is measured as performance?
7. Which costs are displaced to peasants, firms, banks, future budgets, or society?

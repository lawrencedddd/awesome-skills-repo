# Patterns

## Fiscal-Relations Causal Chain
**When to use**: Explaining a local government behavior that looks excessive, distorted, or puzzling.
**How**: Identify revenue assignment, expenditure responsibility, personnel incentives, discretion over land or projects, and the level that bears costs.
**Trade-offs**: Avoid reducing behavior to personal corruption or abstract rational choice before mapping institutions.

## Revenue Squeeze Diagnosis
**When to use**: After reforms that centralize or formalize revenue.
**How**: Ask what revenue sources remain locally controllable, then predict where local governments will shift attention.
**Trade-offs**: Standardization can improve formal control while pushing revenue-seeking into less transparent channels.

## Upward-Looking Government
**When to use**: Transfer payment and project-system cases.
**How**: If money comes from upper levels, track application, approval, earmarking, inspection, and reporting incentives.
**Trade-offs**: Upward accountability can weaken downward responsiveness.

## County-Township Service Chain
**When to use**: Rural education, health, roads, water, and basic public services.
**How**: Map who knows the need, who controls money, who implements, and who is blamed.
**Trade-offs**: The most informed level may not control funds; the fund-controlling level may not know villages.

## Suspended Regime Test
**When to use**: Analyzing post-tax-fee township governance.
**How**: Check whether the township has extraction tasks, service responsibility, discretionary funds, and social connection.
**Trade-offs**: Ending extraction is good, but detachment can harm public-service provision.

## Land-Fiscal-Financial Trinity Loop
**When to use**: Urbanization, local debt, land finance, infrastructure booms.
**How**: Trace land expropriation, land reserve, collateralized borrowing, construction, land conveyance revenue, and tax-base expansion.
**Trade-offs**: The loop creates visible growth and fiscal capacity while accumulating financial and social risk.

## Public-Purpose Test
**When to use**: Evaluating local development strategies.
**How**: Ask whether fiscal capacity serves welfare, equity, and service quality, or whether revenue generation reproduces itself.
**Trade-offs**: High efficiency can worsen outcomes if the target is wrong.

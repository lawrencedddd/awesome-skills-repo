# Module 5: Earnings and the Two-Minute Drill

## Core Idea

The investment thesis must connect the company's story to future earnings and to today's valuation. Lynch's two-minute drill forces a stock idea to become simple, testable, and category-aware.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Earnings first**
  - When to use: when deciding whether price action is meaningful.
  - How: ask whether earnings are rising, sustainable, and large enough to justify the price.
- **P/E relative to growth**
  - When to use: when valuing profitable companies.
  - How: compare P/E to realistic earnings growth. A P/E near sustainable growth can be reasonable; a P/E far above growth needs special justification.
- **Market-cap target sanity check**
  - When to use: when a stock is already highly valued.
  - How: calculate current market cap, imagine the market cap after the hoped-for gain, and ask what earnings would be required to support it.
- **Two-minute drill**
  - When to use: before buying or adding to a stock.
  - How: explain the category, business, growth driver, valuation, balance-sheet risk, and what would disconfirm the story.

## Key Concepts

- **P/E ratio**: price divided by earnings per share.
- **Market capitalization**: share price multiplied by shares outstanding.
- **Earnings growth**: increase in profits, the central driver of long-term stock appreciation.
- **Story**: plain-language thesis for why business results should improve.
- **Sustainable growth**: growth rate the business can plausibly continue without fantasy assumptions.

## Mental Models

- Use **price as the least useful isolated fact**; pair it with earnings and market cap.
- Treat **high P/E as borrowed expectations** that the business must repay with growth.
- Think of **the two-minute drill as a pre-mortem and thesis at once**.

## Anti-patterns

- **No "E" in P/E**: companies without earnings require extra caution and a path to profits.
- **Valuation by share price**: a $10 stock is not cheaper than a $100 stock unless earnings and share count support it.
- **Growth fantasy**: assuming long periods of 30-50 percent growth without a credible runway.
- **Story without numbers**: narrative must connect to earnings and financial condition.

## Worked Example

Lynch's dot-com style sanity check starts with market cap. If a company worth $10 billion must become a tenbagger, it needs to justify a $100 billion value. If a generous P/E of 40 is applied, the company eventually needs roughly $2.5 billion in earnings. The investor then asks whether the business model, market size, margins, and competition make that plausible. This turns excitement into arithmetic.

## Key Takeaways

1. Earnings eventually make or break the stock.
2. P/E must be judged relative to realistic growth.
3. Market cap prevents small-share-price illusions.
4. If you cannot explain the stock in two minutes, keep researching.

# Module 8: Portfolio and Sell Discipline

## Core Idea

Lynch designs portfolios around researched opportunities, not arbitrary diversification. Sell decisions should follow category-specific story deterioration, valuation excess, or superior alternatives.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Own what you can know**
  - When to use: when deciding portfolio size.
  - How: own as many stocks as there are situations where you have an edge and have completed the research. Do not diversify into unknown companies just to diversify.
- **Category allocation**
  - When to use: when balancing risk.
  - How: mix stalwarts, cyclicals, turnarounds, asset plays, and fast growers based on available opportunities and risk tolerance.
- **Watering the flowers, cutting the weeds**
  - When to use: when winners rise and losers fall.
  - How: do not automatically sell winners or add to losers. Recheck the story; add where fundamentals improve and valuation remains sensible.
- **Category-specific sell rules**
  - When to use: after price gains, declines, or new information.
  - How: sell slow growers after modest gains or deteriorating fundamentals; stalwarts after expected gains unless story improves; cyclicals near cycle maturity; fast growers when growth breaks; turnarounds when repair stalls; asset plays when value is recognized or asset case weakens.

## Key Concepts

- **Foolish diversification**: owning unknown companies for the appearance of diversification.
- **Rotation**: moving from a stock whose story worsens or valuation stretches into one whose story improves or valuation is better.
- **Primary vs secondary positions**: larger researched holdings versus smaller monitored opportunities.
- **Risk/reward by category**: each category has different upside/downside profile.

## Mental Models

- Think of **portfolio size as a function of researched ideas**, not a fixed formula.
- Use **story plus price** to decide whether to add, hold, or sell.
- Treat **sell discipline as thesis discipline**, not price-target superstition.

## Anti-patterns

- **Selling solely because a stock doubled**: great companies can continue compounding.
- **Holding solely because a stock is down**: lower price does not repair a broken story.
- **Adding to weeds**: averaging down without improved facts.
- **Overdiversifying beyond knowledge**: reduces the advantage Lynch wants amateurs to exploit.

## Worked Example

For stalwarts, Lynch may seek a series of 30-50 percent gains rather than a tenbagger. For fast growers, he holds as long as earnings and expansion continue. For cyclicals and turnarounds, he rotates out when fundamentals worsen or the price has already anticipated recovery. The same price movement can therefore imply different action depending on category.

## Key Takeaways

1. Diversify only among companies you understand and have researched.
2. Let winners run when the story improves.
3. Do not average down unless the story is stronger, not merely cheaper.
4. Sell rules are category-specific.

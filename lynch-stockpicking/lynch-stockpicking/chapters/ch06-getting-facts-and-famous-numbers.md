# Module 6: Getting Facts and Famous Numbers

## Core Idea

Lynch's homework is practical: use brokers, company calls, annual reports, store visits, and financial statements to verify the story. The key numbers are not decorative; they reveal whether the company can survive, grow, and reward shareholders.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Get the facts**
  - When to use: after a lead passes initial interest.
  - How: read reports, call investor relations, ask brokers useful questions, visit stores or locations, and compare what management says with numbers.
- **Cash and debt check**
  - When to use: for every category, especially turnarounds and cyclicals.
  - How: compare cash to long-term debt, examine debt structure, and ask how long the company can withstand trouble.
- **Inventory red flag**
  - When to use: manufacturers and retailers.
  - How: compare inventory growth with sales growth. Inventory rising faster than sales can indicate weakening demand.
- **Cash flow and asset value**
  - When to use: asset plays, slow businesses, and capital-intensive companies.
  - How: ask how much free cash the assets can produce, what they are worth, and whether management will surface value or waste it.
- **Shareholder reward check**
  - When to use: mature companies and cash-rich firms.
  - How: evaluate dividends, buybacks, and dilution. Buybacks can help if shares are undervalued; dilution can blunt a turnaround.

## Key Concepts

- **Debt-to-equity**: measure of financial leverage.
- **Cash per share**: cash net of debt divided across shares, useful in downside analysis.
- **Book value**: accounting net worth, sometimes useful and sometimes misleading.
- **Hidden assets**: underrecognized real estate, subsidiaries, brands, media properties, tax assets, or natural resources.
- **Cash flow**: cash generated by operations, especially important when accounting earnings are noisy.
- **Inventory**: unsold goods; rising inventory can signal trouble.
- **Profit margin**: profit as a percentage of sales.

## Mental Models

- Use **balance sheet first for survival, income statement second for upside** in troubled companies.
- Think of **inventory as a physical truth serum** for retailers and manufacturers.
- Treat **annual reports as a source of questions**, not polished answers.

## Anti-patterns

- **Ignoring dilution**: a company can turn around while old shareholders gain little.
- **Assuming book value is real value**: assets may be overstated, obsolete, or hard to sell.
- **Missing debt maturity risk**: debt due soon can break a turnaround.
- **Trusting management story without financial confirmation**.

## Worked Example

For a turnaround, Lynch wants to know whether the company can survive creditor pressure. A cash-rich company with little debt can endure mistakes and wait for recovery. A highly leveraged company may be forced to sell shares, sell assets cheaply, or enter bankruptcy. The same business improvement can produce very different shareholder outcomes depending on the starting balance sheet.

## Key Takeaways

1. Good stock research asks practical questions and verifies them with filings.
2. Cash and debt determine survival.
3. Inventory growing faster than sales is a warning sign.
4. Buybacks, dividends, and dilution change the shareholder's outcome.

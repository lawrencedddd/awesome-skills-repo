# Module 7: Rechecking the Story and the Final Checklist

## Core Idea

Owning a stock is not a one-time decision. Lynch rechecks the story every few months and uses category-specific checklists to see whether the original reasons for ownership are improving, intact, or broken.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Rechecking the story**
  - When to use: after purchase and periodically while holding.
  - How: review earnings, expansion, balance sheet, store/product evidence, competitive conditions, and whether the category still fits.
- **Stocks in general checklist**
  - When to use: before buying or during review.
  - How: check P/E versus peers and history, institutional ownership, insider buying, buybacks, balance-sheet strength, and growth record.
- **Fast grower checklist**
  - When to use: for high-growth candidates.
  - How: ask whether growth is 20-25 percent and sustainable, whether expansion is accelerating or slowing, whether debt is manageable, and whether the P/E is near growth.
- **Turnaround checklist**
  - When to use: for troubled companies.
  - How: examine cash, debt, debt structure, operating losses, asset sales, cost cuts, and whether the rescue benefits shareholders.
- **Asset play checklist**
  - When to use: for hidden-value investments.
  - How: estimate asset value, subtract debt, identify catalyst, and check whether the company is taking on debt that dilutes the asset case.

## Key Concepts

- **Story drift**: ownership thesis gradually changes without explicit review.
- **Growth slowdown**: early warning that a fast grower may be revalued as a slower company.
- **Institutional ownership**: low ownership can be positive for neglected growth stories; high ownership may reduce surprise.
- **Catalyst**: event or process that helps hidden value become recognized.

## Mental Models

- Use **"would I buy it today?"** as the clean recheck question.
- Treat **new facts as cards turned over** in the company story.
- Match **warning signs to category**, not generic market noise.

## Anti-patterns

- **Complacency after price gains**: rising price can hide deteriorating fundamentals.
- **Checklist theater**: filling boxes without asking whether the story still works.
- **Ignoring category migration**: fast growers can become stalwarts or slow growers; turnarounds can relapse.
- **Overlooking debt changes**: acquisitions can turn a strong balance sheet into a weak one.

## Worked Example

Lynch describes being pulled back into an airline-related story even as the balance sheet and industry reality worsened. The lesson is to avoid letting price strength or an improved narrative override deteriorating fundamentals. A recheck should ask whether the near-term facts still support the original thesis, especially in cyclicals and leveraged companies.

## Key Takeaways

1. Recheck the story every few months.
2. Use different checklists for different categories.
3. Watch growth rates, debt, expansion pace, and institutional attention.
4. Sell or reduce when facts deteriorate, even if the price has been rising.

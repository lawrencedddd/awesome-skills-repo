# Module 2: Stalking the Tenbagger

## Core Idea

Tenbaggers often begin as obvious business successes in ordinary places. Lynch argues that employees, customers, suppliers, and local observers can notice momentum before analysts do, but the observation must be developed into a financial story.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Stalking the tenbagger**
  - When to use: when a business appears to be spreading, winning customers, or improving before broad recognition.
  - How: verify that the company is public, that the observed product matters to earnings, and that expansion can continue for years.
- **Workplace edge**
  - When to use: when your job exposes you to suppliers, demand shifts, shortages, new products, or industry turnarounds.
  - How: ask what you can know earlier than outsiders, then test whether that knowledge is material and investable.
- **Bottom-line relevance test**
  - When to use: after discovering an exciting product.
  - How: estimate how much the product can move sales and earnings relative to the whole company.

## Key Concepts

- **Tenbagger**: stock that rises tenfold.
- **Edge**: knowledge or observation that gives you better timing or understanding than the average investor.
- **Common knowledge**: widely visible facts that Wall Street may not yet have translated into earnings expectations.
- **Materiality**: whether a product or trend is big enough to matter to company profits.

## Mental Models

- Use **the shopping mall as a research screen**, not a portfolio.
- Think of **employees and suppliers as early-warning sensors** for demand, gluts, shortages, and turnarounds.
- Treat **large-company product hits cautiously**: a blockbuster product may barely move a giant's earnings.

## Anti-patterns

- **Buying every crowded store**: popularity without earnings impact is not enough.
- **Chasing outside your edge**: if specialists know much more than you, your observation may be late or weak.
- **Ignoring company size**: huge companies rarely become tenbaggers unless a major change affects the whole enterprise.

## Worked Example

Lynch contrasts a product that matters to a smaller company with one that barely affects a giant. A successful consumer product can transform a focused company, but the same product inside a massive conglomerate may be too small to change earnings. The first question after a product clue is therefore: "What percentage of this company's earnings can this success influence?"

## Key Takeaways

1. Tenbaggers are often visible before they are fully analyzed.
2. A lead becomes useful only after bottom-line verification.
3. Your best edge is usually close to your real life or work.
4. Company size limits upside; small successful firms can move more than corporate giants.

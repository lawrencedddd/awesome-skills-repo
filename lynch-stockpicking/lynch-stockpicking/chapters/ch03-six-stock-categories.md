# Module 3: The Six Stock Categories

## Core Idea

Lynch's six categories prevent category mistakes. Each type of stock has different upside, risk, evidence, valuation logic, and sell discipline.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Slow growers**
  - When to use: mature companies growing only slightly faster than the economy.
  - How: evaluate dividend safety, balance sheet quality, and whether there is any reason to expect more than modest returns.
- **Stalwarts**
  - When to use: large dependable companies with moderate earnings growth.
  - How: buy at sensible prices, expect moderate gains, and consider rotating after 30-50 percent appreciation unless the story materially improves.
- **Fast growers**
  - When to use: companies expanding earnings rapidly from a smaller base.
  - How: look for strong balance sheets, profits, repeatable expansion, and a long runway.
- **Cyclicals**
  - When to use: autos, airlines, steel, chemicals, commodities, and other businesses tied to cycles.
  - How: buy when conditions are depressed and improving, sell before peak earnings fool the crowd.
- **Turnarounds**
  - When to use: troubled companies where survival and restructuring are central.
  - How: start with cash, debt, burn rate, and evidence that the fix is real.
- **Asset plays**
  - When to use: market price ignores cash, real estate, subsidiaries, natural resources, brands, media franchises, or other hidden assets.
  - How: estimate asset value net of debt and ask what catalyst can surface it.

## Key Concepts

- **Growth rate**: expansion of earnings, sales, or operations; earnings growth matters most.
- **Runway**: room for a company to keep expanding.
- **Cycle**: recurring industry boom-bust pattern.
- **Hidden asset**: valuable asset not reflected in the current market price.
- **Turnaround survival**: ability to avoid bankruptcy or dilution while the business repairs.

## Mental Models

- Use **category before valuation**: what is cheap for a fast grower may be expensive for a slow grower.
- Think of **cyclicals as timing-sensitive**, not buy-and-forget compounders.
- Treat **turnarounds as solvency problems first and upside stories second**.

## Anti-patterns

- **Expecting a stalwart to be a tenbagger**: large companies rarely compound that far without extraordinary change.
- **Buying cyclicals on peak earnings**: low P/E near the top can be a trap.
- **Buying turnarounds without cash runway**: the stock can be diluted or wiped out before recovery.
- **Ignoring debt in asset plays**: creditors have the first claim.

## Worked Example

Lynch treats stalwarts differently from fast growers. A Coca-Cola-like stalwart may be attractive at the right price and may protect a portfolio in hard times, but if it rises 50 percent in a year or two, the investor should consider whether the normal stalwart upside has already been realized. A smaller fast grower with continuing expansion and rising earnings may deserve a longer hold, because the story can keep compounding.

## Key Takeaways

1. Stock category determines expectations and sell rules.
2. Fast growers and turnarounds offer big upside but demand stronger risk checks.
3. Cyclicals require industry-cycle knowledge; valuation ratios can mislead.
4. Asset plays depend on hidden value, debt, and a path to recognition.

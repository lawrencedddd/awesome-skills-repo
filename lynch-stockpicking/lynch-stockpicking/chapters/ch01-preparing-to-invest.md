# Module 1: Preparing to Invest

## Core Idea

Lynch begins by preparing the investor, not the stock list. Before picking stocks, you need a reason to own equities, a realistic temperament, money you can leave invested, and the humility to ignore most market forecasts.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Individual investor edge**
  - When to use: when you encounter a business through work, shopping, family, or local observation before analysts focus on it.
  - How: treat observation as a lead, then verify public status, earnings impact, financial strength, and valuation.
- **Mirror test**
  - When to use: before buying any stock.
  - How: ask whether you own a home or have stable finances, whether you need the money soon, and whether you have the emotional ability to hold through declines.
- **Market timing refusal**
  - When to use: when tempted to ask whether this is a good market.
  - How: shift attention from macro forecasts to company-level facts and valuation.
- **Amateur mindset**
  - When to use: when professional consensus intimidates you.
  - How: focus on situations institutions ignore: small, dull, regional, recently spun off, or too unglamorous for broad coverage.

## Key Concepts

- **Common knowledge**: everyday observations that can become investment leads.
- **Dumb money**: Lynch's affectionate term for individual investors who are not trapped by institutional habits.
- **Street lag**: delay before Wall Street recognizes changes that customers or workers may already see.
- **Temperament**: ability to hold through volatility without reacting to every headline.
- **Long-term ownership**: holding period long enough for earnings and business progress to matter.

## Mental Models

- Think of **a stock as a business interest**, not a price quote.
- Use **home/work/mall observations as radar**, then use financial research as the filter.
- Treat **bearish macro arguments as usually more intelligent-sounding than useful**.

## Anti-patterns

- **Buying because you like the product**: liking a store or product is only a research trigger.
- **Needing near-term money**: stock volatility can force bad selling.
- **Letting professionals define your circle**: institutions may be constrained away from exactly the areas where amateurs can look.
- **Waiting for a perfect market forecast**: you may miss company-specific opportunities.

## Worked Example

Lynch uses consumer products such as L'eggs to show that ordinary observation can precede Wall Street recognition. The key is not "I saw people buying it, so buy the stock." The correct process is: notice the product, identify the public company, ask whether the product is large enough to affect earnings, examine whether the company can expand, and check whether the stock price already assumes the good news.

## Key Takeaways

1. Your edge comes from observed business reality plus homework.
2. Do not invest money you need soon or cannot emotionally risk.
3. Macro timing is a distraction from company-level work.
4. The best amateur opportunities often look too small, dull, or local for institutions.

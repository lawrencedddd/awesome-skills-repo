## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### Use your edge, then do the homework

Lynch's signature idea is that ordinary investors can notice promising companies before Wall Street does because they live inside product markets. A crowded restaurant, beloved retailer, workplace supplier, regional bank, or industry turnaround can be an early clue. The clue is not a buy signal. It is a research lead.

Use this sequence:

1. Notice a product, service, industry shift, or workplace fact.
2. Ask whether the company is public and whether the product matters to earnings.
3. Classify the stock.
4. Build the company story.
5. Check the numbers.
6. Recheck the story every few months.

### Know what kind of stock you own

Lynch's six categories are the routing system for all later analysis:

- **Slow growers** - mature, low-growth, often dividend-oriented companies.
- **Stalwarts** - large quality companies with moderate growth, often useful for 30-50 percent gains.
- **Fast growers** - smaller or expanding firms with the best tenbagger potential.
- **Cyclicals** - companies whose earnings rise and fall with business or commodity cycles.
- **Turnarounds** - troubled companies where survival and repair drive returns.
- **Asset plays** - companies whose hidden assets are worth more than the market recognizes.

Do not apply one category's expectations to another. A fast grower can justify a different checklist from a cyclical; a stalwart is not held for the same reason as an asset play.

### The story is the investment thesis

The "story" is a plain-language explanation of why earnings should improve and why the market has not fully recognized it. If you cannot explain the story briefly, you probably do not understand the stock.

Lynch's two-minute drill asks for:

- what the company does;
- what category it belongs to;
- why earnings should rise;
- what must happen next;
- what could go wrong;
- whether the price is reasonable for the story.

### Earnings drive stocks eventually

Lynch keeps returning to earnings. Stock price is noisy; earnings power is the anchor. A rising stock with no earnings support is speculation. A boring company with steady earnings growth, a modest P/E, a strong balance sheet, and room to expand may be a serious candidate.

Use the P/E ratio relative to growth, not in isolation. A fast grower at a P/E near its sustainable growth rate may be more attractive than a slower grower at an apparently lower multiple. Be especially wary of companies growing 50-100 percent a year; the rate may be unsustainable, and hot industries attract competition.

### Prefer boring, neglected, understandable businesses

The "perfect stock" often sounds dull, ridiculous, disagreeable, or depressing. No-growth industries, niches, spinoffs, insider buying, and share buybacks can create opportunities because sophisticated investors overlook them.

Lynch likes:

- simple businesses;
- durable niches or brands;
- companies using technology rather than selling fashionable technology;
- spinoffs with strong balance sheets;
- insiders buying;
- companies buying back shares;
- low institutional ownership when the story is improving.

### Avoid excitement without economics

Avoid the hottest stock in the hottest industry, "the next" version of a famous winner, diworseification, whisper stocks, companies overly dependent on a single customer, and exciting names without earnings support. Excitement raises expectations, attracts competitors, and makes disappointment expensive.

### Check the balance sheet before the dream

Debt can turn a temporary business problem into bankruptcy. Cash gives a company time to fix itself. Lynch repeatedly asks whether cash exceeds debt, whether inventories are piling up, whether margins and cash flow support the story, and whether share count is shrinking or being diluted.

For turnarounds, survival comes first. For asset plays, creditors come first. For fast growers, financing and expansion discipline matter.

### Buy and sell by story deterioration, not price movement

Do not sell a winner merely because it went up, and do not keep a loser merely because it went down. Recheck the story. Sell when the facts that justified ownership no longer hold, when valuation outruns plausible earnings, when category-specific warning signs appear, or when a better researched opportunity offers a superior risk/reward.

### Ignore macro forecasting

Lynch discourages asking whether "the market" is good. Interest rates, recessions, elections, crashes, and weekly statistics can distract investors from company-level facts. If the company story is sound and price is sensible, market forecasts should not dominate the decision.

# Patterns

## Lead-to-Research Pipeline

**When to use**: you notice a product, store, workplace trend, or industry change.

**How**: identify the public company; determine whether the observation affects earnings; classify the stock; build a two-minute story; check P/E, growth, cash, debt, and competition.

**Trade-offs**: early observation can be valuable, but anecdotal enthusiasm easily outruns financial reality.

## Six-Category Routing

**When to use**: before valuation, purchase, or sell decisions.

**How**: classify the company as slow grower, stalwart, fast grower, cyclical, turnaround, or asset play. Use that category's upside, risk, checklist, and sell rules.

**Trade-offs**: categories can change; reclassify when growth slows, debt rises, or the business shifts.

## Two-Minute Drill

**When to use**: before buying, adding, or defending a holding.

**How**: state the business, category, growth driver, valuation, balance sheet condition, key risk, and what would make you sell.

**Trade-offs**: concise does not mean shallow; the drill should compress research, not replace it.

## P/E-to-Growth Sanity Check

**When to use**: profitable companies with measurable earnings growth.

**How**: compare P/E with sustainable growth. Prefer reasonable P/E relative to growth; be skeptical when P/E assumes years of exceptional growth.

**Trade-offs**: not useful for asset plays, deep cyclicals at trough/peak earnings, or companies without earnings.

## Balance-Sheet Survival Check

**When to use**: turnarounds, cyclicals, and any company in trouble.

**How**: compare cash with debt, study debt maturities, estimate losses, check dilution risk, and ask how long the company can survive.

**Trade-offs**: a strong balance sheet can buy time, but it does not guarantee business recovery.

## Perfect-Stock Screen

**When to use**: looking for neglected opportunities.

**How**: prefer dull names, simple businesses, disagreeable work, spinoffs, no-growth industries, niches, recurring purchases, insider buying, and buybacks.

**Trade-offs**: dullness is useful only with earnings, financial strength, and reasonable price.

## Avoid-List Screen

**When to use**: before buying exciting companies.

**How**: reject hot industries, "next something" stories, diworseification, whisper stocks, single-customer dependence, and exciting names without economics.

**Trade-offs**: some hot companies become great businesses, but the burden of proof is higher.

## Recheck-the-Story Loop

**When to use**: every few months after purchase.

**How**: update earnings, expansion, balance sheet, store checks, competition, category, valuation, and sell triggers.

**Trade-offs**: too much checking can create short-term anxiety; focus on business facts rather than price flickers.

## Category-Specific Sell Discipline

**When to use**: when a holding rises, falls, or news changes.

**How**: sell when category-specific thesis breaks: stalwart reaches expected gain without new story; fast grower slows; cyclical nears peak; turnaround repair fails; asset value is realized or impaired.

**Trade-offs**: selling too early can cut tenbaggers; refusing to sell broken stories can destroy capital.

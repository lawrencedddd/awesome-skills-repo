# Kahneman Cheatsheet

## The One-Minute Bias Audit

1. State the exact question.
2. Name the intuitive answer.
3. Ask what easier question may have been substituted.
4. Write the base rate or reference class.
5. Check sample size and regression to the mean.
6. Look for anchors, vivid examples, stereotypes, and frames.
7. Translate outcomes into equivalent formats.
8. Decide whether the domain permits skilled intuition.

## Forecasting Protocol

1. Define the outcome and time horizon.
2. Select a reference class.
3. Find the distribution of outcomes in that class.
4. Place the current case at the base rate.
5. Adjust only for evidence that is diagnostic and reliable.
6. Express uncertainty as a range or probability.
7. Record the forecast before results arrive.

## Prospect Theory Quick Map

- Gains: often risk averse, especially for high-probability gains.
- Losses: often risk seeking, especially for high-probability losses.
- Small probabilities: often overweighted when described vividly.
- Reference points: determine what counts as a gain or a loss.
- Loss aversion: losses usually have more psychological weight than equal gains.

## When To Trust Intuition

Trust intuition more when:

- the environment has stable patterns,
- feedback is fast and clear,
- the expert has repeated practice,
- outcomes have been tracked honestly.

Trust intuition less when:

- the domain is noisy or low-validity,
- feedback is delayed or ambiguous,
- confidence is based on a coherent story,
- incentives reward persuasion more than accuracy.

## Useful Prompts

- "What is the base rate?"
- "What evidence is missing?"
- "What is the reference point?"
- "How would this look in frequencies rather than percentages?"
- "What would the outside view predict?"
- "If this plan failed, what would the most boring explanation be?"
- "Am I optimizing the experiencing self or the remembering self?"

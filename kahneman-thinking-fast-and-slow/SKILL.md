# Thinking, Fast and Slow

Use this skill when a task involves judgment under uncertainty, decision design, forecasting, risk communication, behavioral economics, cognitive bias diagnosis, or improving reasoning processes. It summarizes Daniel Kahneman's major frameworks without relying on long source quotations.

## Core Operating Model

Kahneman's central contrast is between two modes of cognition:

- **System 1:** fast, automatic, associative, effortless, emotionally colored, and always generating impressions.
- **System 2:** slow, deliberate, effortful, rule-based, and responsible for monitoring, calculation, and self-control.

The practical lesson is not "System 1 is bad" and "System 2 is good." System 1 is indispensable and often skilled. The failure mode is unmonitored System 1: coherent stories, fluent impressions, vivid memories, and easy substitutions get endorsed by a lazy or overloaded System 2.

## Use This Skill To

- Diagnose whether a judgment is driven by anchoring, availability, representativeness, framing, loss aversion, or overconfidence.
- Improve forecasts with base rates, reference classes, regression-to-the-mean checks, and outside-view discipline.
- Design decisions that reduce narrow framing, sunk-cost escalation, misleading confidence, and emotional overreaction to rare events.
- Explain prospect theory, loss aversion, endowment effects, mental accounting, and the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes.
- Distinguish the experiencing self from the remembering self when reasoning about happiness, pain, memory, and life satisfaction.

## Fast Diagnostic Checklist

Before trusting a judgment, ask:

1. **What is the exact target question?** Did intuition answer an easier substitute question?
2. **What is missing?** Apply WYSIATI: what relevant evidence is absent from the story?
3. **What is the base rate?** Start from the reference class before case-specific adjustments.
4. **How valid is the environment?** Expert intuition needs regular patterns and rapid, clear feedback.
5. **Is the sample large enough?** Beware small samples and vivid anecdotes.
6. **What is the reference point?** Choices often depend on gains and losses relative to a status quo.
7. **Is this framed too narrowly?** Repeated risks need portfolio-level policies.
8. **Which self is evaluating?** The experiencing self and remembering self can disagree.

## Main Frameworks

### Two Systems
System 1 generates impressions and impulses. System 2 checks, calculates, inhibits, and reasons. Because System 2 is effortful, it often accepts System 1's output unless a trigger forces inspection.

### WYSIATI
"What You See Is All There Is" captures the mind's tendency to build a confident story from available evidence and neglect absent evidence. Use it whenever a narrative feels complete too quickly.

### Heuristics and Biases
Anchoring, availability, representativeness, substitution, conjunction errors, base-rate neglect, and regression neglect are not random mistakes; they are predictable byproducts of efficient intuitive cognition.

### Overconfidence and Forecasting
Confidence usually tracks coherence, not accuracy. Better forecasts start from the outside view: choose a reference class, examine actual outcomes, then adjust cautiously.

### Prospect Theory
People evaluate outcomes as gains and losses around reference points. Losses loom larger than gains, sensitivity diminishes with distance from the reference point, and small probabilities are often overweighted.

### Two Selves
The experiencing self lives through moments; the remembering self constructs stories and makes future choices. Measures of happiness, pain, and satisfaction depend on which self is being asked.

## Decision Repair Moves

- **Base-rate reset:** Write the reference-class rate before reading the case story.
- **Anchor quarantine:** Generate an independent estimate before seeing suggested numbers.
- **Premortem:** Imagine the plan failed and list plausible causes.
- **Regression check:** Ask how much of the extreme outcome was skill versus noise.
- **Broad frame:** Evaluate repeated small risks as a portfolio.
- **Frame translation:** Rewrite equivalent options in gain, loss, frequency, and absolute-number formats.
- **Decision journal:** Capture assumptions, probabilities, and reasons before outcomes arrive.

# Chapter 2: Where I'm Coming From

## Core Idea
The autobiographical section matters because it shows the mechanism by which Dalio formed his principles: ambition, mistakes, shocks, reflection, institutional learning, and repeated redesign.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Learn through encounters with reality**:
  - When to use: when experience is richer than theory.
  - How: treat each period of life as a test of assumptions and harvest rules from the outcomes.
- **Build a system larger than yourself**:
  - When to use: when success must scale beyond one person's skill.
  - How: move from personal judgment to explicit principles, culture, and tools.

## Key Concepts
- **Failure as feedback**: major errors expose hidden assumptions.
- **Transition risk**: growth and leadership succession reveal whether principles are institutionalized.
- **Scale pressure**: what works personally may break organizationally unless it is systematized.

## Mental Models
- Think of biography as evidence for which rules survived real stress.
- Use extreme setbacks to identify which hidden beliefs were wrong.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating success as proof of permanent competence**: this blinds you to the next regime change.
- **Keeping judgment private**: if only the founder knows how decisions are made, the system cannot scale.

## Worked Example
Dalio's rise, near-collapse, and later institutional success all reinforce the same point: the principles worth keeping are the ones refined through consequences, not the ones that merely sound impressive.

## Key Takeaways
1. Principles built from consequences are more durable than opinions.
2. Success creates new failure modes when scale changes.
3. A strong culture is a memory system for hard-won lessons.

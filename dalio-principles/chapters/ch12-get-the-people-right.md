# Chapter 12: Get the People Right

## Core Idea
Dalio's people principles are blunt: the right people matter more than plans, hiring mistakes are costly, and a high-performing organization continuously trains, evaluates, and sorts.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The who is more important than the what**:
  - When to use: role assignment and delegation.
  - How: put disproportionate attention on who will own the work.
- **Hire right**:
  - When to use: before scaling or trusting a key role.
  - How: test for values, abilities, fit, and believable evidence.
- **Train, test, evaluate, and sort**:
  - When to use: managing talent over time.
  - How: coach where fit is promising, separate when the mismatch is structural.

## Key Concepts
- **Role fit**: alignment between demands and wiring.
- **Evidence over hope**: evaluation should follow observable performance.
- **Sorting**: placing people where they can succeed, including outside the role if needed.

## Mental Models
- Every hiring decision is a machine-design decision.
- Talent quality is cumulative; so are hiring errors.

## Anti-patterns
- **Hiring for charm or comfort**: confusing chemistry with fit.
- **Endless rescue mode**: refusing to acknowledge persistent mismatch.

## Worked Example
The chapter group reinforces Dalio's broader rule that honesty about strengths and weaknesses is a form of respect. Vagueness protects feelings briefly but damages people and the organization over time.

## Key Takeaways
1. Great organizations are built person by person.
2. Evaluation has to be continuous, not annual theater.
3. A role mismatch should trigger redesign, support, or exit - not denial.

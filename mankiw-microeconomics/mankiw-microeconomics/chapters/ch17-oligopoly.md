# Chapter 17: Oligopoly

## Core Idea
This chapter turns Mankiw's introductory microeconomics into a usable reasoning tool for oligopoly. The practical habit is to separate constraints, incentives, equilibrium effects, welfare effects, and distributional effects before giving advice.

## Mental Models
- Start with scarcity, constraints, and the choice margin before debating conclusions.
- Separate positive analysis, what happens, from normative analysis, whether it is desirable.
- Track winners, losers, total surplus, and long-run behavioral response separately.

## Anti-patterns
- **Ignoring opportunity cost**: money cost is only part of economic cost.
- **Confusing legal assignment with economic incidence**: burden follows elasticities, market power, and outside options.
- **Treating a model as the whole reality**: use the model to isolate a mechanism, then state its limits.

## Reference Tables
| Question | Rule of thumb |
|---|---|
| Should an action be taken? | Act when marginal benefit is at least marginal cost. |
| Did a market outcome change? | Identify which curve, constraint, payoff, or information condition changed. |
| Is the outcome efficient? | Compare total surplus before and after the change. |
| Is the outcome fair? | State the distributional criterion separately from efficiency. |

## Worked Example
For a policy, business, or social issue related to this chapter, write down the participants, their choices, the relevant constraint, and the price or payoff they face. Then ask which margin changes, who can adjust more easily, what trades disappear or appear, and whether the result is an efficiency change, a transfer, or both.

## Key Takeaways
1. Use **Cartel** as the first analytical entry point.
2. Do not stop at direction; ask about magnitude, incidence, welfare, and long-run response.
3. Keep empirical mechanism and value judgment separate.

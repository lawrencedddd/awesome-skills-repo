# Glossary

**Applicant-proposing deferred acceptance** - Deferred acceptance with applicants or students as the proposing side, giving that side stronger truth-telling protection (Ch 8, Ch 9).

**Arms race** - Competitive investment that is privately rational but socially wasteful, such as excessive speed competition (Ch 5).

**Blocking pair** - Two participants not matched to each other who would both prefer to be together over their assigned outcomes (Ch 8).

**Boston mechanism** - A school-choice mechanism that favored first-choice rankings and encouraged strategic misrepresentation (Ch 7, Ch 9).

**Bridge donor** - A donor in a kidney-exchange chain who enables future transplants after an earlier link has been completed (Ch 3).

**Clearinghouse** - A centralized marketplace institution that collects preferences and produces matches under shared rules (Ch 4, Ch 8).

**Commodity** - A standardized good whose units can be treated as interchangeable for trading purposes (Ch 2).

**Congestion** - A market jam caused by too many opportunities, messages, or decisions relative to participants' processing capacity (Ch 1, Ch 6).

**Costly signal** - A signal that is credible because it is scarce, costly, or otherwise hard to fake (Ch 10).

**Deferred acceptance** - A matching algorithm in which proposals are made in preference order and receivers tentatively hold their best options until no proposals remain (Ch 8, Ch 9).

**Exploding offer** - An offer with a short deadline that pressures participants to accept before comparing alternatives (Ch 4).

**Free-to-work market** - A market whose rules let participants transact effectively, safely, and voluntarily (Ch 12).

**High-frequency trading** - Automated trading that competes on very small speed differences (Ch 5).

**Kidney exchange** - A matching system that lets incompatible donor-patient pairs exchange donors without buying or selling organs (Ch 3, Ch 11).

**Latency advantage** - A tiny speed edge that can determine priority in electronic markets (Ch 5).

**Market design** - The discipline of designing rules and institutions that make markets work better (Ch 1, Ch 12).

**Market thickness** - The presence of enough participants at the same time to create many possible transactions (Ch 1, Ch 4).

**Matching market** - A market in which both sides must choose each other and price alone does not allocate outcomes (Ch 1).

**Non-directed donor** - A kidney donor who initiates a chain without needing a paired patient to receive a kidney (Ch 3).

**Priority rule** - A rule determining who has precedence in allocation, such as school zones, sibling priority, or order arrival (Ch 5, Ch 9, Ch 12).

**Repugnant transaction** - A transaction some parties want to undertake but others want to prevent (Ch 11).

**Safety** - A market property where participants can act honestly without being strategically punished or exposed to excessive risk (Ch 1, Ch 7).

**Scarce signal** - A limited message or token that conveys special interest because it cannot be sent to everyone (Ch 10).

**Signaling** - Communication designed to convey information about quality, interest, or seriousness (Ch 10).

**Simplicity** - Low cognitive and strategic burden for participants (Ch 7, Ch 9).

**Stability** - A matching property in which no blocking pair has reason to defect together (Ch 8).

**Strategy-proofness** - A mechanism property that reduces or eliminates gains from misrepresenting preferences (Ch 7, Ch 9).

**Two-sided market** - A platform market where each side's participation depends on the other side being present (Ch 2).

**Unraveling** - A market failure where transactions move earlier and earlier because waiting becomes unsafe (Ch 4).

# Chapter 9: Back to School

            ## Core Idea
            School-choice systems allocate public opportunities under priorities, capacity constraints, and family preferences. A good design should let families rank schools honestly while respecting policy priorities and reducing administrative chaos.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **First-choice favoritism**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Opaque priorities**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Multiple uncoordinated offers**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            Under a first-choice-heavy system, a family that loves a selective school may rank it first and lose access to a realistic second choice. Student-proposing deferred acceptance lets the student try the favorite and still compete at later choices by priority.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Public matching mechanisms must be judged by outcomes and strategic burden.
2. Deferred acceptance helps families express preferences more honestly.
3. Priorities are policy choices; algorithms implement them.

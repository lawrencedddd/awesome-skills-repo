# Chapter 5: Too Fast: The Greed for Speed

            ## Core Idea
            Speed is valuable when it helps participants process opportunities, but destructive when tiny speed advantages create arms races. Market designers must decide which speed improvements increase allocation quality and which merely redistribute gains to the fastest.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **Equating faster with better**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Ignoring priority rules**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Letting technology define fairness by accident**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            In high-frequency trading, firms may invest heavily to shave milliseconds from communication routes. If the main effect is to capture trades ahead of slower participants, the social gain may be far smaller than the private reward. Frequent batch clearing can reduce that race.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Speed has to be evaluated by market function.
2. Continuous priority rules can create wasteful speed races.
3. Slowing a market slightly can sometimes make it work better.

# Chapter 4: Too Soon

            ## Core Idea
            Markets can fail before they officially begin when participants rush to transact earlier than others. Early offers and exploding deadlines thin the market by preventing participants from comparing options at the same time.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **First-come, first-served by default**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Allowing exploding deadlines**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Solving congestion by starting earlier**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            In labor markets with early offers, an employer may press a candidate to accept before other employers can interview. The candidate may accept defensively, and other employers respond by moving even earlier. The market unravels.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Timing rules are central to market thickness.
2. Early commitments can be symptoms of an unsafe market.
3. A good start date works only when participants believe others will also wait.

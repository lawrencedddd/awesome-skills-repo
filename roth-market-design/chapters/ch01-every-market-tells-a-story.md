# Chapter 1: Introduction: Every Market Tells a Story

            ## Core Idea
            Markets allocate scarce resources, but many important allocations are matches rather than simple purchases. A good marketplace must become thick, avoid congestion, and make participation safe enough that people can pursue what they actually want.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **Assuming all markets are commodity markets**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Treating rules as interference**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Ignoring strategic gaming**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            A kidney exchange illustrates multiple scarce resources at once: compatible organs, operating rooms, surgeon schedules, transport windows, and legal constraints against kidney sales. Money cannot simply buy the organ, so the market must create compatible matches among donor-patient pairs and coordinate timing tightly enough that each exchange is credible.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Start market design by asking what must be matched, not what can be priced.
2. A market needs thickness, congestion control, and safety; missing any one can break it.
3. Good rules make a market freer to work, not less free.

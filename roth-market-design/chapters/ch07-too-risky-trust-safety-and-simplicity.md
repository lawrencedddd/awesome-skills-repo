# Chapter 7: Too Risky: Trust, Safety, and Simplicity

            ## Core Idea
            Markets need trust and safety so participants can act without excessive fear of fraud, exploitation, strategic penalty, or regret. The best designs often make truthful, straightforward participation the natural move.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **Making participants guess the algorithm**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Relying only on caveat emptor**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Designing for experts**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            Under a school-choice system where ranking a school first gives a major advantage, parents must gamble: list the true favorite and risk losing realistic options, or list a safer school first. A safer design protects truthful ranking.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Safety is partly about incentives, not only fraud prevention.
2. Simpler participation can produce fairer and better matches.
3. Trust mechanisms are core market infrastructure.

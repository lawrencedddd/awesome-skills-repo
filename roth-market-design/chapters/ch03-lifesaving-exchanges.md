# Chapter 3: Lifesaving Exchanges

            ## Core Idea
            Kidney exchange converts an illegal cash market into a legal compatibility market. The key design problem is to create enough thickness and trust for incompatible donor-patient pairs to help one another through cycles and chains.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **Running isolated pools**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Ignoring execution trust**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Treating legal constraints as external**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            Pair A has a donor incompatible with patient A; pair B has the same problem. If donor A is compatible with patient B and donor B is compatible with patient A, a two-way exchange can save both patients. Longer cycles and chains generalize this logic, but increase coordination demands.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Market design can create value without creating a cash market.
2. Bigger pools dramatically improve matching possibilities in constrained medical markets.
3. Logistics, trust, and law are part of the mechanism.

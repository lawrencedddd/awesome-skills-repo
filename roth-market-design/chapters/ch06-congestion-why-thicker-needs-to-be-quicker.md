# Chapter 6: Congestion: Why Thicker Needs to Be Quicker

            ## Core Idea
            A thick market can still fail if participants cannot process opportunities. Congestion appears when communication, attention, evaluation, or commitment is too slow relative to the number of possible transactions.

            ## Key Concepts
            - **Thickness**: Enough participants gather together to create meaningful opportunities.
            - **Congestion**: Participants cannot evaluate or act on opportunities quickly enough.
            - **Safety**: Participants can use the market without excessive risk or strategic distortion.
            - **Rules**: The formal or informal design features that shape behavior.
            - **Strategic behavior**: Participant adaptation to incentives created by the rules.

            ## Mental Models
            - Use **the market as a designed institution** when the current outcome seems natural but is rule-dependent.
            - Use **participant safety** when deciding whether the mechanism helps ordinary users or only sophisticated ones.
            - Think in **failure modes first**: thickness, congestion, safety, legitimacy, and logistics.

            ## Anti-patterns
            - **Celebrating thickness without throughput**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Letting unanswered offers block the market**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.
- **Adding search results without better triage**: It fails because the rule rewards behavior that weakens market function.

            ## Worked Example
            A home-sharing platform may attract many hosts and guests, but if hosts take a day to answer, guests cannot tell whether to wait or search elsewhere. Faster mobile communication, clearer availability, and response expectations turn thickness into transactions.

            ## Key Takeaways
            1. Thickness creates opportunity; congestion determines whether opportunity is usable.
2. Marketplaces often need rules for attention and response.
3. Faster communication helps when it preserves meaningful choice.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### Market Design Starts Where Price Alone Does Not Decide
Use market design when allocation depends on mutual choice, timing, information, legal constraints, or social acceptance rather than only willingness to pay. Colleges choose students and students choose colleges; hospitals choose residents and residents choose hospitals; incompatible kidney donor-patient pairs can help one another only through a structured exchange. In these settings, the question is not merely "what is the price?" but "what rules let participants find acceptable matches?"

### The Three Marketplace Tasks: Thickness, Congestion, Safety
Roth's central diagnostic checklist is:

1. **Make the market thick**: bring enough desirable participants together at the same time so good opportunities exist.
2. **Manage congestion**: let participants evaluate and act on opportunities fast enough that the market does not clog.
3. **Make participation safe and simple**: reduce the need for strategic guessing, deception, premature commitments, or risky workarounds.

A marketplace can fail at any one of these. A thick market that is too slow becomes congested. A fast market that is unsafe rewards gaming. A safe market that is thin gives participants few useful choices.

### Matching Markets Require Mutual Choice
Use the matching-market lens when both sides must choose each other. A buyer can purchase a commodity at the posted price, but a student cannot simply buy admission to a selective school, and an employer cannot hire a worker who refuses the job. Matching markets require rules for proposal, evaluation, acceptance, rejection, and sometimes waiting lists or clearinghouses.

### Commodity Design Turns Matching into Price Clearing
Some markets become simpler when quality can be standardized. Grain grading, coffee exchange rules, securities settlement, and credit-card networks all reduce the need to inspect each counterparty individually. The design move is to identify which attributes can be standardized enough that price can do more work.

### Timing Can Unravel a Market
Markets often unravel when participants rush earlier to avoid being left out. Exploding offers, early admissions pressure, fellowship offers made years in advance, and land-rush behavior all thin the marketplace by forcing decisions before participants can compare alternatives. A market designer should ask: "Are people transacting early because early is better, or because waiting is unsafe?"

### Speed Has a Goldilocks Zone
Too slow creates congestion; too fast can make the market unstable, predatory, or wasteful. High-frequency trading shows how speed advantages can consume resources without improving allocation for most participants. Airbnb-style marketplaces show the opposite: reducing response delays can make a thick market usable. Good design asks which frictions are useful safeguards and which are avoidable bottlenecks.

### Safety Means Truthful Participation Is Not Punished
A market is safer when participants can reveal their real preferences without harming themselves. The Boston school-choice mechanism failed this test because parents had incentives to rank a realistic school first instead of their true favorite. Deferred acceptance mechanisms improve safety by making truthful ranking a better strategy for the proposing side.

### Simplicity Is a Market Feature, Not a Luxury
If successful participation requires strategic expertise, the design favors sophisticated players and punishes ordinary participants. Simplicity is especially important in school choice, medical matching, and organ exchange, where the stakes are high and participants cannot repeat the game many times.

### Clearinghouses Coordinate When Decentralized Offers Fail
A clearinghouse can repair markets with early offers, exploding deadlines, and multiple acceptances by collecting preferences and processing them under shared rules. The National Resident Matching Program is Roth's key example: it creates a thick moment, reduces timing games, and uses an algorithm that makes rank-order lists meaningful.

### Deferred Acceptance as a Safety Device
The deferred acceptance algorithm lets one side propose in preference order while the other side tentatively holds its best current options. Tentative matches can be displaced by better proposals until no further proposals remain. Its value is not mystical optimization; it is that the rules create stability and make truthful preference revelation safer for the proposing side.

### Kidney Exchange Shows Design Under Moral and Legal Constraints
Because buying kidneys is illegal and widely repugnant, kidney exchange designs around money. Incompatible donor-patient pairs can trade compatibility through two-way exchanges, longer cycles, chains, and non-directed donors. The design problem combines logistics, trust, simultaneity, medical compatibility, and incentives to participate in a large enough pool.

### Signaling Conserves Attention When Communication Is Cheap
When applications, messages, or bids become too cheap, receivers cannot tell who is serious. A signal is useful when it is scarce or costly enough to convey information. Labor-market signaling experiments, college admissions interest signals, and targeted advertising all ask the same design question: what communication channel lets serious interest stand out without creating wasteful arms races?

### Repugnant Transactions Are Real Market Constraints
A transaction can be desired by some parties and opposed by others. Repugnance explains why societies ban or restrict markets in kidneys, surrogacy, sex, drugs, horsemeat, and other domains even when voluntary exchange might appear mutually beneficial. Market designers must treat moral limits, legitimacy, and political feasibility as constraints, not as afterthoughts.

### Free Markets Need Rules
Roth's view of a free market is not a free-for-all. A market can operate freely only when its rules allow participants to find, evaluate, and safely complete desirable transactions. The design question is: what rules make the market free to work?

# Chapter 14: Stock Selection for the Defensive Investor

    ## Core Idea
    The defensive investor needs explicit rules: adequate size, strong finances, earnings stability, dividend record, earnings growth, moderate P/E, and moderate price-to-assets.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Relaxing rules for a famous name**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Buying without balance-sheet tests**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Using a checklist mechanically without judgment**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A candidate for the defensive investor must pass minimum size and financial strength, show stable earnings and dividends, demonstrate some growth, and sell at a moderate relationship to earnings and assets.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Defensive selection is rule-based by design.
2. A stock can fail for quality or for price.
3. The checklist reduces avoidable errors.

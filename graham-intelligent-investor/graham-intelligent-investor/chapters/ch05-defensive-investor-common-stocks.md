# Chapter 5: The Defensive Investor and Common Stocks

    ## Core Idea
    The defensive investor can own common stocks, but only through quality, diversification, dividend history, and price limits. The goal is participation in business growth without paying speculative prices.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Buying glamorous growth at any price**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Owning too few issues**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Treating recent earnings as permanent**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A defensive stock list should include large, prominent, conservatively financed companies with long dividend records, purchased at moderate multiples of average earnings. The method sacrifices excitement for durability.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Common stocks are suitable only under disciplined selection.
2. Quality and price must both pass.
3. Diversification protects the defensive investor from single-company mistakes.

# Chapter 18: A Comparison of Eight Pairs of Companies

    ## Core Idea
    Pair comparisons sharpen valuation judgment by contrasting popular vs unpopular, expensive vs cheap, strong story vs strong numbers, and glamour vs protection.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Buying the more admired company automatically**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring competitive mean reversion**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Paying for narrative instead of evidence**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    When two companies in related areas trade at very different valuations, the intelligent investor asks whether the higher price is justified by durable economics or merely by popularity and recent growth.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Relative comparison exposes hidden assumptions.
2. Popularity is often expensive.
3. Mean reversion threatens exceptional profitability.

# Chapter 17: Four Extremely Instructive Case Histories

    ## Core Idea
    Case histories show how obvious warning signs can precede disaster: weak coverage, accounting oddities, promotional finance, leverage, and glamour pricing.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Ignoring tax and accounting anomalies**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Trusting reported earnings without coverage tests**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Buying glamorous names with fragile finances**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A bond with weak interest coverage, poor tax evidence, and deteriorating operating results may still trade near stronger issues for a while. Graham's point is that the investor had enough evidence to avoid the loss before default.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Bad outcomes often announce themselves in the figures.
2. Avoidance can be the best return.
3. Case studies train pattern recognition.

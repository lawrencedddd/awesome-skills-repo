# Introduction: What This Book Expects to Accomplish

    ## Core Idea
    Graham frames investing as a discipline of policy, attitude, and evidence rather than prediction. The intelligent investor needs a sound framework, historical memory, and emotional control.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Treating the book as a get-rich-quick manual**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring market history**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A saver who buys strong common stocks regularly across a full cycle may begin at an unfortunate market level and still earn a respectable long-run result, because disciplined accumulation can offset bad timing better than forecasts can.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Use history to discipline expectations.
2. Separate an investment program from market promises.
3. Build a policy you can follow under stress.

# Chapter 4: General Portfolio Policy: The Defensive Investor

    ## Core Idea
    The defensive investor's first decision is asset allocation between high-grade bonds and common stocks. Graham favors a disciplined range rather than all-or-nothing market calls.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Going 100% stocks because the market feels safe**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Going 100% cash from fear**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Reaching for yield in weak credits**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A defensive investor may keep stock exposure between roughly one-quarter and three-quarters of the portfolio, adjusting only when valuations and circumstances clearly justify it. The rule protects against both euphoria and panic.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Asset allocation is a policy decision before it is a market opinion.
2. High-grade fixed income is ballast, not excitement.
3. Ranges beat heroic forecasts.

# Chapter 1: Investment versus Speculation

    ## Core Idea
    Investment is an operation based on thorough analysis, safety of principal, and adequate return; anything lacking those elements is speculative. Graham's first task is to force the investor to know which activity he is actually doing.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Calling speculation investment**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Using margin casually**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Buying because a stock is popular or rising**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    An investor may hold a diversified stock portfolio for long-term participation in business results. A speculator may trade based on near-term market action. Graham does not ban speculation; he insists it be recognized, limited, and never confused with investing.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Name the activity honestly before judging it.
2. Speculation is most dangerous when disguised as investment.
3. Expected results should match effort, skill, and risk.

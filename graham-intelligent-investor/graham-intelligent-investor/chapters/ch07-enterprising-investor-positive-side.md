# Chapter 7: Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: The Positive Side

    ## Core Idea
    Enterprising investing is not prediction for its own sake; it is disciplined search for specific situations where price is low relative to value, assets, earnings power, or corporate events.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Chasing growth labels**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Mistaking activity for enterprise**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring downside protection**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A stock selling below net current asset value may be unattractive as a story but attractive as arithmetic. The investor seeks a group of such bargains so individual disappointments are offset by statistical advantage.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Enterprise means extra work, not extra optimism.
2. Look for measurable value gaps.
3. Special situations require competence and diversification.

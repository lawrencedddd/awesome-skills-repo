# Chapter 13: A Comparison of Four Listed Companies

    ## Core Idea
    Side-by-side comparison reveals how price, quality, growth, profitability, balance sheet strength, and dividend record interact. Graham teaches valuation by contrast rather than slogans.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Choosing the best company without asking price**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Choosing the cheapest company without asking quality**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Letting growth overwhelm valuation**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    Two companies may look superior by growth and profitability but sell at much higher multiples; two others may look dull but offer better value relative to assets, dividends, and earnings. The analyst compares both merit and price.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Comparison clarifies judgment.
2. A superior company can be an inferior purchase at the wrong price.
3. Cheapness needs quality checks.

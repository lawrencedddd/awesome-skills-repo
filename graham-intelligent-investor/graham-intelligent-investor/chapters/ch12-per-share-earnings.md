# Chapter 12: Things to Consider About Per-Share Earnings

    ## Core Idea
    Reported earnings require skepticism. The investor must adjust for dilution, special charges, accounting choices, cyclicality, and the temptation to capitalize temporary results.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Valuing a company on a single peak year**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring warrants or convertibles**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Accepting accounting presentation uncritically**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    If a company reports attractive per-share earnings while issuing warrants or convertibles that can expand the share count, the investor should analyze earnings on a diluted basis rather than accept headline EPS.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Earnings are inputs, not facts beyond question.
2. Average and adjusted earnings are safer than peak earnings.
3. Accounting can flatter; analysis must normalize.

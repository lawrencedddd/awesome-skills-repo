# Chapter 3: A Century of Stock-Market History

    ## Core Idea
    The investor should treat market history as a guardrail against extrapolation. High prices and high enthusiasm tend to reduce future returns, while pessimism can create opportunity.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Projecting recent market returns forward**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring valuation at purchase**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Mistaking a bull market for skill**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    When a major index stands at an elevated relationship to earnings, dividends, and bond yields, Graham's response is not precise market timing but caution: reduce return expectations, preserve balance, and avoid commitments that require rosy assumptions.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Market level matters because price determines future return.
2. History does not forecast exactly, but it humbles forecasts.
3. Bull markets teach the wrong lessons if valuation is ignored.

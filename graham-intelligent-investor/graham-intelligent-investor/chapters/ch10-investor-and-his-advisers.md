# Chapter 10: The Investor and His Advisers

    ## Core Idea
    Advisers can be useful, but the investor remains responsible for policy and judgment. Advice should be evaluated by competence, incentives, conservatism, and fit with the investor's needs.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Outsourcing responsibility**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Treating salesmanship as counsel**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Buying new issues on recommendation alone**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A broker may provide market access and information, but may also be compensated by transactions. The intelligent investor separates execution help from fiduciary judgment and asks how the adviser is paid.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Advice is useful only when incentives are understood.
2. Do not buy what you cannot independently justify.
3. The investor cannot delegate temperament.

# Chapter 16: Convertible Issues and Warrants

    ## Core Idea
    Convertibles and warrants combine investment and speculative features. The investor must inspect what is given up for conversion privilege and whether the terms favor buyer or issuer.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Accepting lower yield without valuing the option**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring subordination**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Treating warrants as safe investments**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A convertible bond may offer a lower coupon and weaker claim than a straight bond. The investor pays for conversion privilege through foregone income and seniority, so the option must be worth the sacrifice.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Hybrid securities require decomposing the parts.
2. Conversion privilege is not free.
3. Warrants are often speculative unless price creates protection.

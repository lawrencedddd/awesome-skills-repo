# Chapter 2: The Investor and Inflation

    ## Core Idea
    Inflation is a real threat, but fear of inflation does not justify overpaying for stocks or chasing fashionable hedges. The investor should use diversification, price discipline, and realistic expectations.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Assuming stocks always protect against inflation**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Overpaying for inflation stories**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring bond purchasing-power risk**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    Common stocks can sometimes protect purchasing power better than fixed claims, but not at any price. If the market prices stocks as though inflation protection is guaranteed, the supposed hedge can become the source of loss.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Inflation protection depends on valuation.
2. No asset class is a magic shield.
3. Plan for purchasing power without abandoning margin of safety.

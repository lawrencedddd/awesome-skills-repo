# Chapter 15: Stock Selection for the Enterprising Investor

    ## Core Idea
    The enterprising investor seeks above-average results through disciplined, research-heavy methods such as bargain issues, special situations, and neglected securities, not through market prophecy.

    ## Key Concepts
    - **Intrinsic value**: A conservative estimate of business value based on facts rather than market mood.
    - **Margin of safety**: The discount or protection that absorbs analytical error and adverse developments.
    - **Earnings power**: Normalized ability of a business to generate profits through cycles.
    - **Financial strength**: Balance-sheet and coverage protection against adversity.
    - **Temperament**: The investor's ability to follow policy despite market emotion.

    ## Mental Models
    - Use **businesslike investing** when deciding whether you are buying an ownership interest or a price movement.
    - Use **price-paid discipline** because future return is shaped by the price you pay today.
    - Use **avoidance as skill** when a category or security requires too many favorable assumptions.

    ## Anti-patterns
    - **Overconfidence in forecasting**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Concentrating in story stocks**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.
- **Ignoring the need for many trials**: It fails because it substitutes hope, fashion, or sales pressure for analysis and safety.

    ## Worked Example
    A group of unpopular but financially sound secondary companies selling at low multiples may offer better odds than a celebrated growth favorite. The method depends on valuation, diversification, and patience.

    ## Key Takeaways
    1. Enterprise requires measurable edge.
2. Neglect can create opportunity.
3. Diversify bargain strategies because individual outcomes are uncertain.

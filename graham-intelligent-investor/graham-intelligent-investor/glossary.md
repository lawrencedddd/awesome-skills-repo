# Glossary

**Adequate return** - A return sufficient for the risk and effort, not necessarily an extraordinary return (Ch 1).
**Asset allocation** - The policy split among stocks, bonds, and cash (Ch 4).
**Bargain issue** - A security selling for less than conservatively appraised value (Ch 7, Ch 15).
**Businesslike investing** - Treating securities as ownership interests or claims, not as trading tokens (Ch 8, Ch 20).
**Convertible issue** - A bond or preferred stock exchangeable into common stock under specified terms (Ch 16).
**Defensive investor** - An investor seeking satisfactory results with minimal effort, complexity, and risk of serious error (Ch 4, Ch 5, Ch 14).
**Dilution** - Reduction in per-share value or earnings from warrants, convertibles, or new shares (Ch 12, Ch 16).
**Dollar-cost averaging** - Regular fixed-amount purchases through market fluctuations (Introduction, Ch 8).
**Earnings power** - Normalized earning capacity across a business cycle (Ch 11, Ch 12).
**Enterprising investor** - An investor willing and able to do extra work for potentially better results (Ch 6, Ch 7, Ch 15).
**Intrinsic value** - Conservative estimate of value based on business facts rather than market quotation (Ch 11, Ch 20).
**Investment** - Operation based on analysis, safety of principal, and adequate return (Ch 1).
**Margin of safety** - Protection created by buying below conservative value or with ample coverage (Ch 20).
**Mr. Market** - Graham's metaphor for market quotations as emotional offers, not commands (Ch 8).
**Net current asset value** - Current assets minus total liabilities, used in bargain-stock analysis (Ch 7, Ch 15).
**New issue** - A newly offered security, often sold under conditions favorable to promoters and sellers (Ch 6, Ch 10).
**Price/earnings ratio** - Price divided by earnings; Graham uses it as a valuation discipline, especially against average earnings (Ch 5, Ch 14).
**Safety of principal** - Reasonable protection against permanent loss under adverse conditions (Ch 1, Ch 20).
**Security analysis** - Examination of securities through earnings, assets, coverage, financial strength, and valuation (Ch 11).
**Speculation** - Activity lacking Graham's full investment safeguards, especially reliance on price movement or future optimism (Ch 1).
**Warrant** - A security giving the right to buy common stock, usually speculative and dilutive (Ch 16).

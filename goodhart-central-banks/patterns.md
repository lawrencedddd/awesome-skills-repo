# Patterns

## Clearing Creates Hierarchy
**When to use**: When a banking system grows dense enough that banks settle claims against each other continuously.
**How**: Look for the institution that standardizes settlement, enforces rules, and starts monitoring member behavior.
**Trade-offs**: Improves coordination; concentrates authority and can become the seed of central banking.

## Information Failure Produces Supervision
**When to use**: When depositors or markets cannot reliably tell healthy banks from weak ones.
**How**: Expect entry controls, examinations, membership rules, and some certifying authority to emerge.
**Trade-offs**: Raises confidence; reduces pure market freedom.

## Private Clubs Drift Toward Public Functions
**When to use**: When private banking associations begin managing systemic problems.
**How**: Track how they move from mutual convenience to rule-setting, emergency support, and quasi-regulatory roles.
**Trade-offs**: Flexible at first; may prove too weak or partial in generalized panic.

## Support Requires Oversight
**When to use**: When a system expects emergency lending or rescue.
**How**: Pair support capacity with collateral rules, surveillance, and behavioral constraints.
**Trade-offs**: Limits moral hazard; widens institutional reach.

## Payments Stability Forces Collective Action
**When to use**: When one bank's disruption threatens settlement for many others.
**How**: Ask who has both the incentive and authority to keep the system functioning during stress.
**Trade-offs**: Makes decentralization harder to preserve; creates systemic dependence on a coordinator.

## Crisis Lending Becomes Central Banking
**When to use**: When a private institution repeatedly lends in panic to preserve the system.
**How**: Notice when ad hoc support hardens into standing expectation, then into formal public or central authority.
**Trade-offs**: Stabilizes crises; makes the institution politically and economically indispensable.

## Moral Hazard Is a Design Problem, Not an Exit Option
**When to use**: When critics argue that support should disappear because it encourages risk.
**How**: Distinguish between the need for support and the need to redesign incentives around it.
**Trade-offs**: More realistic than abolition; demands institutional discipline.

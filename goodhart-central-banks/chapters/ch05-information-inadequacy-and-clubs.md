# Chapter 5: Information Inadequacy Leading to the Emergence of "Clubs"

## Core Idea
Information problems make pure market discipline too weak and too slow. Goodhart argues that banking systems respond by forming clubs with membership rules, standards, and mutual oversight.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Information inadequacy**:
  - When to use: when asking why outsiders cannot discipline banks effectively.
  - How: note the cost of learning bank quality before and during crisis.
- **Clubs as governance response**:
  - When to use: when private associations arise to reduce uncertainty.
  - How: track entry rules, conduct rules, and arbitration functions.

## Key Concepts
- Asymmetric information
- Membership standards
- Mutual monitoring

## Mental Models
- When information is expensive, institutions emerge to economize on trust.
- Clubs are partial solutions that often foreshadow public authority.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming depositors can efficiently sort strong from weak banks.
- Treating entry and conduct rules as unnecessary distortions.

## Worked Example
Goodhart shows that clubs arise because market participants themselves need structured ways to distinguish reputable institutions from dangerous ones. The move toward supervision starts inside the market.

## Key Takeaways
1. Information failure is a structural banking problem.
2. Governance emerges when trust cannot be cheaply decentralized.
3. Clubs are important transitional institutions in the history of central banking.

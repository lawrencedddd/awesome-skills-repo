# Chapter 7: Why Banks Need a Central Bank

## Core Idea
This is the book's decisive chapter. Goodhart argues that because banks jointly provide payments services and portfolio-management services, they generate liquidity and confidence needs that eventually require a central bank.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Banks' twin functions**:
  - When to use: when explaining why banks are systemically special.
  - How: analyze banks as both payments institutions and balance-sheet transformers.
- **Central bank support logic**:
  - When to use: when a bank crisis threatens both depositor wealth and credit provision.
  - How: ask who can lend, certify, and coordinate quickly enough to preserve the system.
- **Support requires supervision**:
  - When to use: when crisis lending creates moral hazard concerns.
  - How: pair backstop functions with oversight and access conditions.

## Key Concepts
- Payments system
- Portfolio management
- Lender of last resort

## Mental Models
- The institution that preserves payments continuity under panic becomes systemically central.
- A central bank solves not only liquidity shortage but coordination failure.

## Anti-patterns
- Focusing only on note issue or inflation and missing systemic support functions.
- Demanding rescue without accepting surveillance or rules.

## Worked Example
Goodhart emphasizes that bank failures do not just injure depositors; they can cut credit lines to borrowers and disrupt payments for the wider economy. That wider spillover is why a central backstop becomes hard to avoid.

## Key Takeaways
1. Banking combines functions that make decentralized failure unusually costly.
2. The need for support is rooted in structure, not sentiment.
3. Once support exists, supervision and central authority deepen together.

# Chapter 6: Private Insurance?

## Core Idea
Goodhart tests whether private insurance can replace central-bank or public backstop functions. His answer is cautious: private schemes can help, but contagion, confidence effects, and information problems sharply limit them.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Private insurance under contagion**:
  - When to use: when proposing market insurance as a substitute for central support.
  - How: test how the insurer would behave during system-wide stress rather than isolated failure.
- **Confidence externality**:
  - When to use: when insurance ratings or visible weakness may trigger runs.
  - How: examine whether the act of differentiating risk worsens instability.

## Key Concepts
- Deposit insurance
- Contagion risk
- Confidence-sensitive ratings

## Mental Models
- Insurance is hardest exactly where it is most needed: during shared panic.
- A private solution may reveal information in ways that intensify runs.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating insurance as if it were ordinary line-by-line risk pricing.
- Ignoring the public-confidence dimension of bank protection.

## Worked Example
Goodhart's discussion of private insurance proposals shows that even thoughtful market-based solutions tend to run into a core problem: banks are interdependent, and visible reassessment can destabilize the system.

## Key Takeaways
1. Private insurance is limited by systemic correlation and confidence effects.
2. Shared panic is qualitatively different from idiosyncratic failure.
3. Support and supervision remain hard to privatize fully.

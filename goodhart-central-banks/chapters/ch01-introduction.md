# Chapter 1: Introduction

## Core Idea
Goodhart frames the book as a response to revived free-banking arguments. His aim is to show that central banks emerged through the natural development of banking systems rather than through arbitrary intervention.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Natural evolution of central banking**:
  - When to use: when evaluating whether central banks are accidental or necessary.
  - How: examine which banking functions repeatedly required centralized coordination.
- **Banking-specific institutional logic**:
  - When to use: when generic free-market arguments are applied to banking.
  - How: test whether payments, confidence, and support needs make banking structurally different.

## Key Concepts
- Free banking critique
- Central-bank rationale
- Historical institutional development

## Mental Models
- Start from banking functions, not ideology.
- If the same support and coordination needs recur historically, they are probably structural.

## Anti-patterns
- Importing free-trade logic into banking without adjustment.
- Treating central banks as purely artificial state inventions.

## Worked Example
Goodhart positions himself against the claim that central banks are merely inflationary intrusions into an otherwise self-equilibrating system. The whole book is designed to test that claim against history and banking theory.

## Key Takeaways
1. Banking must be analyzed on its own institutional terms.
2. Historical development is evidence, not background decoration.
3. The question is functional necessity, not ideological preference.

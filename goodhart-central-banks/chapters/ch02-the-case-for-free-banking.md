# Chapter 2: The Case for Free Banking

## Core Idea
Goodhart reconstructs the best arguments for free banking, especially through Bagehot and Henry Thornton, and shows where those arguments understate banking-specific instability and policy needs.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Competition analogy**:
  - When to use: when the argument is that banking should look like other competitive industries.
  - How: test whether banking's liabilities, confidence dependence, and externalities break the analogy.
- **Central-bank inflation bias critique**:
  - When to use: when central banks are portrayed as inherently inflationary.
  - How: separate the genuine risk of discretionary expansion from the separate question of crisis coordination.

## Key Concepts
- Bagehot
- Henry Thornton
- Inflationary tendency critique

## Mental Models
- A strong opposing case should be taken seriously before it is rejected.
- The core test is whether private incentives alone generate system stability.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating anti-central-bank arguments as obviously foolish.
- Collapsing the issue of inflation bias into the issue of financial stability.

## Worked Example
Goodhart uses classical debates to show that the free-banking case is intellectually respectable but incomplete. It captures some costs of centralization while downplaying liquidity, contagion, and information problems.

## Key Takeaways
1. Free-banking arguments are strongest when focused on overreach and inflation risk.
2. They weaken when banking is treated like an ordinary industry.
3. Stability functions must be analyzed separately from ideological preference.

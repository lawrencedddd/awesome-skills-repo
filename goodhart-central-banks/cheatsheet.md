# Cheatsheet

## Goodhart's Fast Test

| If the system has this problem | Expect this institutional response |
|---|---|
| Banks cannot trust each other's positions fully | Clearinghouse rules and monitoring |
| Depositors cannot identify weak banks cheaply | Supervision, membership standards, certification |
| Panic threatens payment continuity | Lender-of-last-resort support |
| Support is repeatedly expected | Centralized authority and oversight |
| Rescue creates risk-taking incentives | Prudential constraints, collateral rules, discipline |

## Decision Rules

1. **Do not ask whether central banking is ideologically pure** - ask which banking function needed to be performed.
2. **Treat payments instability as system-critical** - it pushes hardest toward coordination.
3. **When information is costly, pure market discipline weakens** - expect supervisory institutions to arise.
4. **When support becomes necessary, laissez-faire banking stops being self-sufficient**.
5. **Moral hazard argues for design, not for pretending rescue will never happen**.

## Free Banking Stress Test

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Who monitors members well enough to sustain confidence? | Free banking depends on credible discipline |
| Who coordinates settlement under strain? | Payments failure spreads fast |
| Who lends against temporarily illiquid assets? | Liquidity crises do not wait for theory |
| Who distinguishes one weak bank from a system panic? | Without this, contagion dominates |

## Tells and Smells

| If you see this | Suspect this |
|---|---|
| Private coordination bodies taking on emergency roles | Proto-central banking is already emerging |
| Calls for support but resistance to oversight | Hidden demand for public backstop without accountability |
| Strong faith in competition alone | Banking-specific frictions are being understated |
| Repeated panic responses by the same institution | Central-bank function exists even if not fully formalized |

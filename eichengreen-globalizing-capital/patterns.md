# Patterns

## Fixed Rates Fail When Domestic Politics Reject the Required Adjustment
**When to use**: When a currency peg looks credible in markets but fragile in politics.
**How**: Ask whether wages, employment, banking stability, and voter tolerance can bear the contraction or restraint needed to defend the peg.
**Trade-offs**: Strong for interwar gold, Bretton Woods strains, and euro tensions; should not ignore cases where external support buys time.

## Restoring an Old Regime Without Its Old Conditions Is Dangerous
**When to use**: When policymakers try to re-create a previously successful monetary arrangement.
**How**: Compare not just rules but supporting institutions, labor politics, franchise breadth, financial structure, and policy priorities.
**Trade-offs**: A powerful historical test; may understate what targeted redesign can salvage.

## Capital Mobility Exposes Inconsistency Faster
**When to use**: When governments try to keep contradictory promises under more open financial conditions.
**How**: Track whether mobile capital is testing the trilemma corner the regime claims to occupy.
**Trade-offs**: Clarifies crisis timing; can over-credit markets and under-credit domestic fragility if used alone.

## International Systems Break on the Adjustment Question
**When to use**: When a regime seems stable until deficits or surpluses persist.
**How**: Ask who is expected to deflate, revalue, borrow, or accept reserve loss - and whether that burden is politically sustainable.
**Trade-offs**: Central to Eichengreen's analysis; should be paired with liquidity and banking considerations.

## Floating Solves Some Constraints but Creates New Coordination Problems
**When to use**: When the end of a peg is presented as a clean solution.
**How**: Check volatility, misalignment, spillovers, and crisis transmission after the move to floating.
**Trade-offs**: Avoids romanticizing floating; also avoids pretending pegs are automatically superior.

## Crises Are Better Read as Diagnostics Than Accidents
**When to use**: When a major episode seems like a one-off shock.
**How**: Ask what hidden assumption of the regime the crisis exposed - liquidity, credibility, banking strength, political cohesion, or reserve adequacy.
**Trade-offs**: Great for extracting lessons; may oversimplify multi-causal events if pushed too hard.

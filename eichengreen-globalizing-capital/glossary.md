# Glossary

**Adjustment mechanism** - The way prices, output, reserves, capital flows, and policy changes eliminate balance-of-payments deficits and surpluses. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Bimetallism** - A monetary arrangement using both gold and silver, central to early debates before universal gold adoption. (Ch 2)

**Bretton Woods** - The post-World War II system of pegged-but-adjustable exchange rates, capital controls, and dollar-centered liquidity. (Ch 4)

**Capital controls** - Restrictions on cross-border financial flows used to preserve policy autonomy or exchange-rate stability. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Capital mobility** - The ability of funds to move across borders quickly, intensifying discipline and crisis transmission. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Impossible trinity / trilemma** - The constraint that countries cannot simultaneously have fixed exchange rates, open capital markets, and independent monetary policy. (Ch 1)

**Interwar gold standard** - The post-World War I attempt to reconstruct gold-based monetary order under changed political and economic conditions. (Ch 3)

**Managed floating** - A regime where exchange rates move but authorities still intervene or guide outcomes rather than leaving them fully free. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Pegged-but-adjustable** - Bretton Woods-style exchange rates that are fixed in normal times but can be changed under strain. (Ch 4)

**Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)** - International reserve assets created to supplement global liquidity under Bretton Woods constraints. (Ch 4)

**Sterling crisis** - Episodes where confidence in the pound and the UK's external position pressured the wider international system. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Trilemma corner** - A chosen policy combination that sacrifices one element of the impossible trinity in order to keep two others. (Ch 8)

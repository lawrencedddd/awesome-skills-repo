# Chapter 1: Introduction

## Core Idea
Eichengreen introduces the international monetary system as the institutional glue holding national economies together. He frames the book around how regimes create order, what trade-offs they impose, and why they fail.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **International monetary system as binding architecture**:
  - When to use: when defining the subject itself.
  - How: think in terms of exchange rules, adjustment, liquidity, and cooperation.
- **Impossible trinity**:
  - When to use: when comparing policy regimes.
  - How: map each era's sacrifice among fixed rates, capital mobility, and monetary autonomy.

## Key Concepts
- Exchange-rate regime
- Adjustment
- Trilemma

## Mental Models
- Monetary regimes are political bargains, not just technical blueprints.
- Regime choice is always constrained; there is no free combination of all objectives.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating the system as a neutral background rather than an active policy structure.
- Assuming all countries can pursue all monetary goals simultaneously.

## Worked Example
The chapter establishes that monetary arrangements succeed when domestic institutions and international rules fit each other, and fail when that fit breaks.

## Key Takeaways
1. The international monetary system channels national policy choices.
2. The trilemma is a practical historical constraint.
3. Regimes should be judged by both economics and political sustainability.

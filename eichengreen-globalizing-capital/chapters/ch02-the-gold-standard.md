# Chapter 2: The Gold Standard

## Core Idea
The pre-1914 gold standard looked stable not because it was an automatic machine, but because a particular historical setting made its discipline politically acceptable and operationally workable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Gold standard as historically specific institution**:
  - When to use: when someone treats gold as timelessly self-evident.
  - How: inspect the political, institutional, and financial conditions that sustained it.
- **Peripheral instability under core stability**:
  - When to use: when judging whether a regime was stable for everyone.
  - How: compare the center with the periphery rather than reading only the core-country record.

## Key Concepts
- Bimetallism
- Convertibility
- Lender of last resort under gold

## Mental Models
- A regime can be stable for core powers while remaining fragile at the edges.
- Gold convertibility depends on social willingness to prioritize external balance.

## Anti-patterns
- Romanticizing pre-1914 gold as pure automatic adjustment.
- Ignoring institutional support like central-bank practice and international cooperation.

## Worked Example
Eichengreen shows that the system's apparent automaticity was underwritten by policy choices, solidarity, and a narrower democratic base than later eras would tolerate.

## Key Takeaways
1. Gold worked under specific pre-1914 conditions.
2. Stability relied on politics as much as specie rules.
3. Peripheral fragility complicates the myth of universal success.

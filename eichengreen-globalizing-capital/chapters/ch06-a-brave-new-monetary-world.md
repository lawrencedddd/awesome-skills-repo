# Chapter 6: A Brave New Monetary World

## Core Idea
This chapter surveys the post-Cold War and pre-global-financial-crisis environment: Asian crisis lessons, mounting instability, global imbalances, the euro, and intensified currency competition.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Liberalization with fragility**:
  - When to use: when capital opening is treated as unambiguously stabilizing.
  - How: ask whether institutions, reserves, and banking systems are ready for volatile flows.
- **Global imbalance buildup**:
  - When to use: when one country's surpluses and another's deficits seem easy to sustain.
  - How: examine reserve accumulation, consumption patterns, and dollar dependence.

## Key Concepts
- Asian crisis
- Global imbalances
- Euro and currency competition

## Mental Models
- Financial openness amplifies both discipline and fragility.
- Surplus-deficit relationships can look comfortable until confidence changes.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating reserve accumulation or external borrowing as costless indefinitely.
- Assuming Europe's monetary integration removed crisis risk rather than transformed it.

## Worked Example
Eichengreen links the Asian crisis and later imbalances to a wider regime in which liberalized capital and incomplete institutional adaptation created recurrent instability.

## Key Takeaways
1. Liberalization without adequate support structures is dangerous.
2. Global imbalances can persist longer than expected but not forever.
3. The euro changed the geography of monetary risk rather than abolishing it.

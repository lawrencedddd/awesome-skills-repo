# Chapter 4: The Bretton Woods System

## Core Idea
Bretton Woods was a historically informed compromise: enough exchange-rate stability to support trade and reconstruction, enough domestic policy space to avoid the social devastation of the interwar years.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Pegged-but-adjustable compromise**:
  - When to use: when analyzing mid-century monetary stability.
  - How: focus on fixed rates, occasional realignment, and restricted capital mobility.
- **Domestic autonomy through controls**:
  - When to use: when asking how countries regained room for internal management.
  - How: see capital controls as functional design rather than mere repression.

## Key Concepts
- Convertibility
- SDRs
- Dollar problem

## Mental Models
- Bretton Woods traded some market freedom for policy manageability.
- The system's strength was also its eventual weakness once capital mobility rose and dollar strains deepened.

## Anti-patterns
- Reading Bretton Woods as a pure return to fixed-rate orthodoxy.
- Forgetting that the system depended on constraints absent later.

## Worked Example
Eichengreen shows how wartime planning, postwar reconstruction, the EPU, convertibility issues, and dollar tensions shaped a regime more politically realistic than interwar gold.

## Key Takeaways
1. Bretton Woods was designed to avoid interwar mistakes.
2. Capital controls were central, not incidental.
3. The dollar's privileged position contained future instability.

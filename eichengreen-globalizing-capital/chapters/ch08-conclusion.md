# Chapter 8: Conclusion

## Core Idea
Eichengreen's conclusion is that no international monetary system is permanently self-stabilizing. Regimes survive when their rules fit the political economy of the states inside them; they fail when that fit breaks.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Regime fit**:
  - When to use: when judging whether a monetary order is durable.
  - How: compare exchange-rate commitments, capital conditions, and domestic political constraints.
- **Adaptation over restoration**:
  - When to use: when policymakers propose returning to a previous system.
  - How: ask what changed in politics, finance, and global structure since the earlier regime.

## Key Concepts
- Institutional fit
- Adaptation
- Historical contingency

## Mental Models
- Monetary orders are always provisional solutions.
- The key historical lesson is not to idealize any one past arrangement out of context.

## Anti-patterns
- Searching for a single permanently optimal regime.
- Treating historical success as proof of future replicability.

## Worked Example
The conclusion gathers the book's recurring insight: gold, Bretton Woods, floating, and regional systems each worked to the extent that they aligned external commitments with domestic tolerances and available policy tools.

## Key Takeaways
1. There is no timeless best regime.
2. International monetary stability requires political compatibility as well as technical design.
3. Crises are warnings about misfit, not just noise.

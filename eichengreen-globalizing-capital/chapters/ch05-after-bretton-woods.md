# Chapter 5: After Bretton Woods

## Core Idea
The collapse of Bretton Woods did not end international monetary problems; it changed their form. Floating rates, regional pegs, and developing-country vulnerabilities created a new regime mix with new failure modes.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Floating as constrained freedom**:
  - When to use: when post-1970s exchange regimes are described as fully liberated.
  - How: examine intervention, misalignment, and coordination pressures.
- **Regional integration under mobility**:
  - When to use: when studying Europe after Bretton Woods.
  - How: track the snake, EMS, and the push toward deeper monetary integration.

## Key Concepts
- Floating exchange rates
- EMS
- Developing-country experience

## Mental Models
- The end of one regime expands options but not immunity.
- Post-Bretton Woods systems still wrestle with adjustment and credibility.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming floating rates automatically solve external imbalance problems.
- Treating European monetary integration as a purely technical exchange-rate story.

## Worked Example
The chapter follows 1970s and 1980s floating, Europe's path through the snake and EMS, and the difficulties faced by developing countries under volatile capital conditions.

## Key Takeaways
1. Floating replaced one set of constraints with another.
2. Europe sought stability by building stronger regional frameworks.
3. Developing countries often faced the harshest version of the new volatility.

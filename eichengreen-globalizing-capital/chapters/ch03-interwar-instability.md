# Chapter 3: Interwar Instability

## Core Idea
The interwar period is Eichengreen's great cautionary tale: policymakers tried to reconstruct the gold standard after World War I without the political and economic foundations that had once supported it.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Restoration without foundations**:
  - When to use: when an old regime is revived after structural change.
  - How: compare the new era's politics, labor markets, debts, and financial conditions to the old one.
- **Depression as regime test**:
  - When to use: when severe downturn meets rigid monetary commitment.
  - How: ask whether the exchange regime amplifies or eases the contraction.

## Key Concepts
- New gold standard
- Great Depression
- Sterling and dollar crises

## Mental Models
- The harder the domestic cost of external defense, the more brittle the regime.
- Monetary orthodoxy collapses when electorates and banks cannot bear the adjustment.

## Anti-patterns
- Explaining interwar collapse as simple policy incompetence without regime mismatch.
- Assuming credibility can override mass politics indefinitely.

## Worked Example
The chapter tracks failed restoration, banking crises, the breakdown of sterling and then the dollar link, and the turn toward managed floating as old rules became untenable.

## Key Takeaways
1. Interwar gold failed because the world had changed.
2. Depression pressure exposed the political limits of rigid pegs.
3. Monetary collapse and banking collapse interacted destructively.

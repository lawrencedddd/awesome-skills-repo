## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### International monetary systems are political-institutional orders, not just exchange-rate rules
- Eichengreen's core move is to treat the monetary system as an arrangement that binds national economies together through exchange-rate norms, adjustment procedures, liquidity provision, and capital-mobility rules.
- A regime works not only because its formal rules look coherent, but because domestic politics, central-bank practice, and international cooperation make those rules tolerable.

### The "impossible trinity" is real in practice
- Countries cannot simultaneously enjoy fixed exchange rates, full capital mobility, and independent monetary policy.
- Different eras choose different corners of this trilemma.
- Eichengreen repeatedly shows that crises erupt when governments pretend they can hold more than the triangle allows.

### The gold standard worked under specific historical conditions
- It was not a timeless self-equilibrating machine.
- It depended on limited mass democracy, a willingness to subordinate domestic employment goals to external convertibility, and significant elite consensus.
- Its success before 1914 therefore does not prove easy replicability under later political conditions.

### Interwar failure came from restoring the old system without the old foundations
- Policymakers tried to revive gold-standard discipline after World War I.
- But social structure, labor politics, debt burdens, and domestic policy priorities had changed.
- The restored gold standard failed because the supporting political and economic environment no longer existed.

### Bretton Woods was a compromise between external discipline and domestic autonomy
- The system used pegged-but-adjustable rates, capital controls, and international support mechanisms to give states more room for domestic management than the classical gold standard had allowed.
- Its design reflected the lessons of the interwar catastrophe.
- It was more flexible than gold but less permissive than later floating regimes.

### Capital mobility changes everything
- As controls weaken and cross-border finance grows, governments lose room to maintain inconsistent policies.
- Rising mobility increases the speed of discipline but also the speed of contagion.
- Many later crises are best understood as episodes where mobile capital exposed regime contradictions.

### Adjustment problems are central, not incidental
- Every system must decide how deficit and surplus countries adjust.
- A regime becomes fragile when the burden of adjustment falls too asymmetrically or politically painfully on one side.
- This is why debates over gold, dollar convertibility, European pegs, and global imbalances all recur.

### Credibility is not enough if domestic politics cannot bear the cost
- Policymakers often defend fixed rates in the name of credibility.
- Eichengreen's historical lesson is that credibility unsupported by social tolerance, banking resilience, and political legitimacy is brittle.
- Markets test not just economic arithmetic but political staying power.

### Floating does not abolish international monetary problems
- The end of Bretton Woods did not deliver a frictionless equilibrium.
- Floating rates reduce one constraint but leave countries exposed to volatility, misalignment, crisis transmission, and coordination failures.
- Post-Bretton Woods history is therefore not "problem solved," but "different problems, differently distributed."

### Crisis episodes reveal the regime's hidden assumptions
- The Asian crisis, euro tensions, global imbalances, subprime spillovers, and sovereign-debt episodes all expose what a system assumed about liquidity, cooperation, and the adjustment burden.
- Eichengreen's method is to read crises diagnostically: what contradiction did this episode uncover?

# Cheatsheet

## Regime Test

| If a country wants this | It usually must give up this |
|---|---|
| Fixed exchange rate + monetary independence | Full capital mobility |
| Fixed exchange rate + open capital markets | Monetary autonomy |
| Monetary independence + open capital markets | A rigid fixed exchange rate |

## Fast Questions

1. What trilemma corner is this regime trying to occupy?
2. Who bears the adjustment burden if imbalances persist?
3. Are domestic politics compatible with the external commitment?
4. How mobile is capital under this regime?
5. Is crisis support available if banking or balance-of-payments pressure rises?

## Tells and Smells

| If you see this | Suspect this |
|---|---|
| A government defending a peg despite recession and social strain | Political tolerance may fail before reserves do |
| Calls to restore a past monetary order | The supporting historical conditions may be missing |
| Large imbalances plus reserve-currency dependence | Adjustment conflict is building |
| Rapid liberalization with weak institutions | Crisis transmission risk is rising |
| Floating rates praised as full freedom | Coordination and volatility problems may be understated |

## Era Defaults

| Era | Core trade-off |
|---|---|
| Classical gold standard | External discipline over domestic autonomy |
| Interwar restoration | Old rules under new politics |
| Bretton Woods | Stability with constrained capital mobility |
| Post-Bretton Woods | More monetary autonomy, more volatility and crisis diversity |

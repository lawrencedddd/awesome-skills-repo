# Chapter 12: The Ultimate Inflation

## Core Idea
Hyperinflation is not ordinary price instability but a destruction of belief in money itself. Galbraith uses Germany to show the depth of political and social injury monetary collapse can inflict.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hyperinflation as civic trauma**:
  - When to use: when explaining why inflation fear can become near-religious.
  - How: connect collapsing money to collapsing contracts, savings, and trust.
- **Memory overreaction**:
  - When to use: when later policy becomes excessively anti-inflationary.
  - How: trace doctrine back to remembered catastrophe.

## Key Concepts
- Weimar inflation
- Savings destruction
- Political backlash

## Mental Models
- Hyperinflation rewrites the public imagination more than most monetary events.
- The fear it leaves behind can shape policy long after circumstances change.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating all inflation as if it were Weimar.
- Ignoring how social trauma distorts later policy judgment.

## Worked Example
Galbraith's account emphasizes not only the economics of inflation but the way it enters memory as humiliation, dispossession, and moral disorder.

## Key Takeaways
1. Hyperinflation destroys more than prices.
2. Its memory can dominate future policy debate.
3. Monetary trauma is historically specific but politically portable.

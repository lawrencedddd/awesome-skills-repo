# Chapter 5: Of Paper

## Core Idea
Galbraith argues that the history of government paper money belongs especially to the Americans. Paper money is controversial because it makes visible what coinage hides: money works because institutions can make claims acceptable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Paper money as sovereign expedient**:
  - When to use: when explaining why states issue paper.
  - How: connect it to fiscal need, political authority, and practical shortage of specie.
- **Parity through acceptance**:
  - When to use: when asking why paper can circulate at full value.
  - How: examine convertibility, tax authority, legal tender, and general confidence.

## Key Concepts
- Government note issue
- Specie shortage
- Monetary confidence

## Mental Models
- Paper money is shocking mainly because it makes monetary convention plain.
- The real question is not whether paper is "intrinsically" valuable but whether the regime behind it is credible.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating paper money as fraudulent by definition.
- Ignoring how often metallic scarcity forced innovation.

## Worked Example
Colonial and early American practice demonstrates that paper finance was not an eccentric side note but a recurring solution to real economic and fiscal constraints.

## Key Takeaways
1. Paper money emerges from necessity as much as ideology.
2. It can work well before it fails badly.
3. Acceptance depends on institutions, not paper quality.

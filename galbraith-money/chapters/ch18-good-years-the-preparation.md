# Chapter 18: Good Years: The Preparation

## Core Idea
The two decades after 1948 appear in Galbraith's telling as unusually benign because institutions, policy tools, and doctrine were better aligned than before. Stability was built, not gifted.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Managed prosperity**:
  - When to use: when explaining sustained postwar stability.
  - How: look at demand management, regulation, and institutional support together.
- **Preparation through institutions**:
  - When to use: when good performance seems natural.
  - How: identify the policy architecture that made crisis less likely.

## Key Concepts
- Postwar growth
- Macro stabilization
- Bretton Woods preparation

## Mental Models
- Good eras are often the product of remembered disaster plus better tools.
- Stable prosperity can be historically exceptional, not baseline.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming the postwar decades prove markets self-stabilize.
- Forgetting the regulatory and intellectual scaffolding behind the calm.

## Worked Example
Galbraith highlights how the language of GNP, planning, and steady expansion reflected a world newly confident that deep slump and monetary chaos were not inevitable.

## Key Takeaways
1. Stability is institutional work.
2. Postwar calm reflected deliberate design as much as luck.
3. Prosperity can hide the seeds of later stress.

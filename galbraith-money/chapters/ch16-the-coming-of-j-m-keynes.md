# Chapter 16: The Coming of J. M. Keynes

## Core Idea
Keynes is the book's decisive intellectual turning point. He made it possible to think of unemployment and inadequate demand as problems to be managed rather than punishments to be endured.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Demand management**:
  - When to use: when private spending collapses and the economy does not self-correct fast enough.
  - How: use public action and monetary-fiscal support to stabilize output and employment.
- **Macro over moralism**:
  - When to use: when older doctrines frame slump as necessary correction.
  - How: shift from virtue language to aggregate demand analysis.

## Key Concepts
- John Maynard Keynes
- Effective demand
- Public intervention

## Mental Models
- Unemployment can persist because spending is too low, not because wages are insufficiently punished.
- Monetary and fiscal policy are tools, not sacred limits.

## Anti-patterns
- Waiting for automatic restoration of full employment.
- Treating government action as distortion by default.

## Worked Example
Galbraith presents Keynes not as a minor refinement but as a reorganization of what responsible policy could mean in an industrial democracy.

## Key Takeaways
1. Keynes changes the legitimate aims of policy.
2. Demand shortfalls require active response.
3. Monetary history after Keynes cannot be read by pre-Keynesian rules alone.

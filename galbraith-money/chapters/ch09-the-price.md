# Chapter 9: The Price

## Core Idea
Money is tied to wealth, prestige, and social ranking. Galbraith uses the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries to show how monetary order and social inequality reinforce each other.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Monetary order as class environment**:
  - When to use: when money debates appear abstract.
  - How: ask how they shape fortunes, debt burdens, and access to security.
- **Price stability for whom?**:
  - When to use: when restraint is praised as universal good.
  - How: identify whose assets and social position that stability protects.

## Key Concepts
- Wealth display
- Property power
- Distributional effects

## Mental Models
- Monetary arrangements are never detached from social hierarchy.
- Prices and stability carry class meaning because they affect claims differently.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating money history as detached from inequality.
- Assuming policy benefits are evenly shared.

## Worked Example
Galbraith's treatment of the era emphasizes how much easier it was to praise monetary order when one stood among those already protected by property and status.

## Key Takeaways
1. Monetary policy has social winners and losers.
2. Respectable finance often rests on unequal cushions.
3. The history of money is partly a history of who gets security.

# Chapter 11: The Fall

## Core Idea
The pre-1914 confidence in gold and orthodox monetary order was shattered by war and the strains that followed. Galbraith treats this as a collapse of belief as much as a collapse of mechanism.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Orthodoxy undone by events**:
  - When to use: when a supposedly final system suddenly becomes impossible.
  - How: examine what emergency conditions reveal about hidden fragility.
- **Gold as brittle order**:
  - When to use: when a regime appears stable only in calm conditions.
  - How: ask whether it can survive war, capital stress, and mass politics.

## Key Concepts
- Gold convertibility
- Wartime suspension
- System breakdown

## Mental Models
- Monetary systems often look strongest right before their historical limits become obvious.
- The more universal the doctrine sounds, the more dramatic its fall can be.

## Anti-patterns
- Reading prewar gold order as timeless success.
- Ignoring the way political shock changes what a monetary system can bear.

## Worked Example
Galbraith uses the post-1914 break to show that old assurances about gold's permanence were contingent, not eternal.

## Key Takeaways
1. Monetary stability can depend on unusually calm historical conditions.
2. War is a brutal audit of monetary doctrine.
3. Once broken, orthodoxy rarely returns unchanged.

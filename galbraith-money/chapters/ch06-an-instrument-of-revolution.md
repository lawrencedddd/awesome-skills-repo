# Chapter 6: An Instrument of Revolution

## Core Idea
War and political rupture make paper money unavoidable. Galbraith treats revolutionary finance as proof that monetary orthodoxy gives way quickly once survival is at stake.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Emergency over orthodoxy**:
  - When to use: when governments suspend prior monetary scruples.
  - How: read wartime paper issue as a response to impossible fiscal needs.
- **Depreciation as political memory**:
  - When to use: when later generations become hard-money zealots.
  - How: link anti-inflation sentiment to remembered monetary collapse.

## Key Concepts
- Continental finance
- Wartime depreciation
- Revolutionary necessity

## Mental Models
- Paper issue is often the only available bridge between military need and weak tax capacity.
- The memory of failed paper outlives the conditions that made it necessary.

## Anti-patterns
- Condemning emergency finance without asking what alternative existed.
- Forgetting that successful independence sometimes required financially ugly methods.

## Worked Example
Galbraith shows that revolutionary paper money was not embraced because it was ideal; it was embraced because the colonies needed a spendable claim when no superior instrument was available.

## Key Takeaways
1. Emergencies make monetary innovation politically acceptable.
2. Inflation trauma leaves long ideological shadows.
3. Monetary history cannot be separated from war finance.

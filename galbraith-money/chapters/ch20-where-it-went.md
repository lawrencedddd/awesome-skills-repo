# Chapter 20: Where It Went

## Core Idea
Galbraith turns to the unraveling of postwar monetary order, especially the strains on the dollar, the growth of Eurodollars, and the limits of fixed exchange commitments in a changing world economy.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Reserve currency contradiction**:
  - When to use: when one national currency anchors the wider system.
  - How: track how international demand for that currency eventually undermines its own convertibility promises.
- **Success breeding imbalance**:
  - When to use: when a stable order starts to fail from its own scale.
  - How: examine trade flows, capital accumulation abroad, and confidence shifts.

## Key Concepts
- Dollar overvaluation
- Eurodollars
- Gold drain

## Mental Models
- International monetary systems break first at the point where national policy and global role diverge.
- Reserve status is both privilege and burden.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating dollar dominance as a permanent law.
- Assuming fixed exchange arrangements can ignore changing productivity and trade patterns.

## Worked Example
Galbraith describes the accumulation of dollars abroad and the pressure on US gold as a slow revelation that the system's center could no longer promise what the system expected.

## Key Takeaways
1. Global success can overstrain a national currency.
2. International imbalances eventually become monetary crises.
3. The end of one system usually begins while it is still publicly defended.

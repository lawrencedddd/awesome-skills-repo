# Chapter 13: The Self-Inflicted Wounds

## Core Idea
Galbraith's treatment of Britain is a study in policy error made respectable by orthodoxy. Defending monetary prestige can become a way of engineering needless economic pain.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Prestige-driven policy error**:
  - When to use: when governments defend monetary status at high domestic cost.
  - How: separate symbolic restoration from practical economic benefit.
- **Deflation as chosen pain**:
  - When to use: when unemployment and contraction are accepted for doctrinal ends.
  - How: identify who is being asked to absorb the burden of "discipline."

## Key Concepts
- Return to gold
- British rigidity
- Deflationary burden

## Mental Models
- Not all suffering imposed by monetary policy is necessary suffering.
- Orthodoxy can wound an economy in the name of restoring confidence.

## Anti-patterns
- Confusing symbolic financial status with national economic health.
- Treating deflation as morally cleaner than inflation regardless of cost.

## Worked Example
Britain's experience lets Galbraith show that monetary pain can be self-authored when leaders pursue prestige and tradition over employment and adjustment.

## Key Takeaways
1. Deflation can be an avoidable policy choice.
2. Gold restoration can serve sentiment more than prosperity.
3. Respectability is a poor substitute for workable policy.

# Chapter 1: Money

## Core Idea
Galbraith begins by stripping money of false mystery. Money matters because it stores and transmits claims, but its practical meaning depends on what people think it will buy and whether they trust it to hold value long enough to use it.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Money as accepted claim**:
  - When to use: when asking what makes money "real."
  - How: look at acceptance, purchasing power, and institutional backing, not only the physical object.
- **Purchasing power over nominal amount**:
  - When to use: when judging what money is worth across time.
  - How: track what a unit of money can actually command, not what number is printed on it.

## Key Concepts
- Confidence
- Purchasing power
- Monetary illusion

## Mental Models
- Think of money as belief organized by institutions.
- Ask what protects the holder from erosion through inflation or collapse.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating money as value itself rather than a claim on value.
- Forgetting that idle cash can lose meaning quickly when prices move.

## Worked Example
Galbraith opens with an anecdotal contrast between possession of money and the way inflation quietly erodes it. The point is practical: money only matters through what it can still buy.

## Key Takeaways
1. Money is useful because people accept it.
2. Nominal sums can mislead if purchasing power changes.
3. Confidence is always part of the monetary mechanism.

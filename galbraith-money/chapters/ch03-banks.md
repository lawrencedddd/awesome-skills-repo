# Chapter 3: Banks

## Core Idea
Banks arise not as passive vaults but as institutions that transform deposits, credit, and payment. Galbraith treats them as one of the core generators of money.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Banking as money creation**:
  - When to use: when distinguishing banking from simple safekeeping.
  - How: show how deposits and loans produce additional means of payment.
- **Credit as monetary extension**:
  - When to use: when explaining how commerce scales beyond available coin.
  - How: trace how trust in bank liabilities substitutes for metal.

## Key Concepts
- Deposit banking
- Credit creation
- Liquidity confidence

## Mental Models
- Banks expand trade by expanding claims.
- A bank's strength rests on confidence that not all claims will be redeemed at once.

## Anti-patterns
- Describing banks as mere intermediaries.
- Ignoring how fragile the system becomes when confidence shifts.

## Worked Example
Galbraith's survey of banking origins shows a recurring pattern: once bank liabilities are accepted as payment, banks effectively join states and mints as money creators.

## Key Takeaways
1. Banks are central monetary actors, not side institutions.
2. Credit creation is powerful because it widens the means of exchange.
3. The same mechanism that supports trade also creates run risk.

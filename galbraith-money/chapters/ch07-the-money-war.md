# Chapter 7: The Money War

## Core Idea
After inflation and collapse, societies become newly devout about sound money. Galbraith reads early US monetary conflict as a struggle among memory, class interest, and institution building.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Post-inflation hard-money reaction**:
  - When to use: when political actors suddenly become monetary purists.
  - How: trace the stance back to recent depreciation and creditor interests.
- **Money as constitutional politics**:
  - When to use: when monetary arrangements look like technical law.
  - How: read them as fights over federal power, regional interest, and social trust.

## Key Concepts
- Sound money
- Creditor-debtor conflict
- Early republic finance

## Mental Models
- Hard money is rarely just a technical preference.
- Monetary settlement is always also a political settlement.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming stable money rhetoric serves everyone equally.
- Treating constitutional monetary design as socially neutral.

## Worked Example
The post-revolution American turn toward restraint shows how quickly one monetary trauma becomes the moral framework for the next era's politics.

## Key Takeaways
1. Inflation memories shape doctrine for decades.
2. Monetary institutions distribute power between groups and regions.
3. "Soundness" often reflects whose losses policymakers most fear.

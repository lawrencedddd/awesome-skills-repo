# Chapter 2: Of Coins and Treasure

## Core Idea
For much of history, money and metal were tightly linked. Galbraith shows why coinage solved practical exchange problems while also inviting mythology around treasure, sovereignty, and the supposed purity of precious metal.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Coin as standardized trust**:
  - When to use: when explaining why metal money beat barter and raw bullion.
  - How: emphasize state certification of weight and fineness.
- **Treasure mentality**:
  - When to use: when rulers or societies confuse hoarded metal with general prosperity.
  - How: distinguish stockpiled treasure from a functioning monetary and trading system.

## Key Concepts
- Coinage
- Sovereign stamp
- Precious metal hierarchy

## Mental Models
- Coins reduce verification costs.
- Metallic money still relies on authority and convention, not just substance.

## Anti-patterns
- Equating national wealth with accumulated bullion.
- Assuming metallic money removes politics from monetary life.

## Worked Example
The chapter uses early coinage history to show that even the most "solid" money required social endorsement. Metal was convenient, but authority made it circulate smoothly.

## Key Takeaways
1. Coinage is a political technology as well as a metallic one.
2. Treasure and prosperity are not the same.
3. Gold's prestige starts early and lingers long.

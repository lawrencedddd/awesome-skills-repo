# Chapter 14: When the Money Stopped

## Core Idea
The Great Depression is monetary collapse experienced by ordinary life. Galbraith treats bank failures and contraction as events that made abstract monetary doctrine morally and politically untenable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Collapse through contraction**:
  - When to use: when money and credit disappear from circulation.
  - How: trace how falling confidence, bank failure, and shrinking demand reinforce each other.
- **Liquidity failure as social disaster**:
  - When to use: when technical banking problems become mass unemployment.
  - How: connect financial stoppage to household fear and reduced spending.

## Key Concepts
- Great Depression
- Banking panic
- Monetary contraction

## Mental Models
- A monetary system can fail by vanishing, not only by inflating.
- Fear is economically active: it tightens behavior and worsens collapse.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating bank failures as isolated firm events.
- Underestimating how quickly lost confidence reduces real activity.

## Worked Example
Galbraith's emphasis is that once money stops moving, the result is not a neat technical reset but a generalized social emergency.

## Key Takeaways
1. Deflationary collapse can be as historically decisive as inflation.
2. Bank failure is macroeconomic, not merely financial.
3. A functioning money system is part of social order itself.

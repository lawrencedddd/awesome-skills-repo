# Glossary

**Bank-created money** - Money created when banks extend credit and generate deposits that function as spendable claims. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Bretton Woods** - The post-World War II international monetary order organized around the dollar and formal exchange arrangements. (Ch 17, Ch 18, Ch 20)

**Central bank** - The institution that manages reserves, liquidity, and often the banking system and currency standard. (Ch 10)

**Convertibility** - The promise that one monetary form can be exchanged for another, often paper for specie or currency for gold. (Ch 5, Ch 11)

**Deflation** - Falling prices and contracting money or credit conditions, often accompanied by debt distress and unemployment. (Ch 13, Ch 14)

**Eurodollars** - Dollar deposits and claims held outside the United States banking system, central to later international monetary strain. (Ch 20)

**Free banking** - A looser US banking order with broad chartering and recurring instability, especially before stronger central control. (Ch 8)

**Gold standard** - A monetary system linking currency value and convertibility to gold, often praised for discipline and criticized for rigidity. (Ch 7, Ch 11, Ch 13)

**Greenbacks** - US government paper money issued during the Civil War era. (Ch 8)

**Hyperinflation** - Extreme inflation that destroys monetary credibility, purchasing power, and social confidence. (Ch 12)

**John Law** - Early eighteenth-century financier whose banking and paper-money experiments in France illustrate both monetary creativity and speculative ruin. (Ch 4)

**Keynesian economics** - The view that demand can fall short for long periods and that public action is required to stabilize output and employment. (Ch 16, Ch 19)

**Paper money** - State-issued money whose value depends on acceptance, law, and confidence rather than direct metallic content. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Reserve currency** - A national currency widely used internationally for trade, reserves, and settlement, bringing both privilege and strain. (Ch 20)

**Sound money** - A moralized label for monetary restraint, often attached to metallic standards or anti-inflation policy. (Ch 7, Ch 15)

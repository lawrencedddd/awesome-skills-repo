## Core Frameworks & Historical Lens

### Money is not a thing first; it is a social arrangement
- Galbraith treats money less as metal and more as a system of accepted claims.
- The key question is not "what substance backs it?" but "why do people accept it, and under what institutions does that acceptance break?"
- This makes confidence, law, politics, and enforcement central to monetary history.

### Three recurrent creators of money
- **Mints** create coin.
- **Governments and treasuries** create paper claims.
- **Banks** create credit.
- A large share of monetary history is the struggle over which of these creators is trusted, constrained, feared, or politically empowered.

### Monetary virtue is usually retrospective
- Hard-money morality rises after inflation.
- Monetary flexibility becomes respectable after depression, war, or banking collapse.
- Galbraith repeatedly shows that what counts as "sound money" depends less on timeless principle than on the pain people most recently endured.

### Gold is discipline, myth, and trap
- The gold standard looks orderly because it imposes visible external restraint.
- It also transmits deflation, punishes employment, narrows policy choice, and invites reverence well beyond its practical merits.
- Galbraith is skeptical of gold not because it lacks historical importance, but because it so often gets confused with monetary wisdom itself.

### Banking is creative and dangerous
- Banks do not merely store money; they generate spendable claims through lending.
- That creative power supports trade and growth.
- The same power produces speculation, leverage, runs, and recurrent demands for rescue.

### Crises teach better than theory
- A running Galbraith theme is that policy improves only after catastrophe has made old doctrines impossible to defend.
- Inflations, panics, depressions, and exchange crises are educational events for institutions, though never educational enough to prevent the next failure.

### Inflation and deflation are political, not only technical
- Inflation changes debt burdens, wages, savings, and class claims.
- Deflation does the same, often more brutally.
- Monetary policy therefore always has distributive consequences, even when presented as neutral technique.

### Central banks attract reverence they do not always deserve
- Galbraith treats institutional prestige with suspicion.
- The Federal Reserve and similar bodies are not outside politics or beyond error.
- Technical vocabulary and procedural dignity can obscure plain institutional failure.

### Keynes matters because he made mass unemployment governable
- Keynes is the pivot from passive orthodoxy to active macroeconomic management.
- The key move is to reject the idea that economies naturally self-correct fast enough to spare ordinary people prolonged ruin.
- Demand management, public spending, and tolerance for a more elastic monetary order become intellectually and politically defensible through Keynes.

### Reserve currencies and international systems carry their own contradictions
- A national currency used internationally gains power and prestige.
- It also accumulates external pressures, convertibility strain, and eventually credibility tests.
- Bretton Woods and the dollar system illustrate how success breeds instability when the rest of the world relies on one country's liabilities.

# Chapter 35: The Short-Run Trade-off Between Inflation and Unemployment

## Core Idea
There is a short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment, but no stable long-run trade-off once expectations adjust.

## Frameworks Introduced

## Mental Models
- Separate **real variables** from **nominal variables** before explaining causality.
- Distinguish **accounting identities** from behavioral theories.
- Ask whether the question is about the **long run** or the **short run**; Mankiw's answer often changes with the time horizon.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating GDP as welfare**: GDP is useful but omits leisure, inequality, nonmarket production, and environmental quality.
- **Ignoring expectations**: inflation, interest rates, and policy effects depend on what people expected before the policy.
- **Mixing identities with policy claims**: an identity must hold, but it does not by itself say what caused what.

## Worked Example
A central bank that slows money growth can reduce inflation, but if people expected higher inflation, output may fall and unemployment rise during adjustment.

## Key Takeaways
1. The long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment.
2. Supply shocks worsen the inflation-unemployment trade-off.
3. Credible policy can reduce the cost of disinflation.

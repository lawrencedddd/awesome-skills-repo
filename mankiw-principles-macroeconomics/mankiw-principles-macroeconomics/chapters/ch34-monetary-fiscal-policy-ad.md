# Chapter 34: The Influence of Monetary and Fiscal Policy on Aggregate Demand

## Core Idea
Monetary and fiscal policy affect aggregate demand, but their size and timing depend on interest rates, multiplier effects, crowding out, and policy lags.

## Frameworks Introduced

## Mental Models
- Separate **real variables** from **nominal variables** before explaining causality.
- Distinguish **accounting identities** from behavioral theories.
- Ask whether the question is about the **long run** or the **short run**; Mankiw's answer often changes with the time horizon.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating GDP as welfare**: GDP is useful but omits leisure, inequality, nonmarket production, and environmental quality.
- **Ignoring expectations**: inflation, interest rates, and policy effects depend on what people expected before the policy.
- **Mixing identities with policy claims**: an identity must hold, but it does not by itself say what caused what.

## Worked Example
An increase in government purchases raises aggregate demand directly, but higher income raises money demand, interest rates rise, and investment may fall partly offsetting the stimulus.

## Key Takeaways
1. Monetary policy shifts AD through interest rates.
2. Fiscal policy shifts AD directly and through induced consumption.
3. Policy activism faces uncertain lags and magnitudes.

# Chapter 33: Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

## Core Idea
Short-run fluctuations can be analyzed with aggregate demand and aggregate supply, where output and prices adjust after demand or supply shocks.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **AD slope**: price-level changes affect spending through wealth, interest-rate, and exchange-rate effects.
  - When to use: when analyzing macro data, long-run growth, monetary policy, open-economy adjustment, or short-run fluctuations.
  - How: define the aggregate variable, write the accounting identity or model, identify the shock or policy, then trace equilibrium effects.
- **AS distinction**: long-run aggregate supply is vertical at natural output; short-run aggregate supply slopes upward.

## Mental Models
- Separate **real variables** from **nominal variables** before explaining causality.
- Distinguish **accounting identities** from behavioral theories.
- Ask whether the question is about the **long run** or the **short run**; Mankiw's answer often changes with the time horizon.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating GDP as welfare**: GDP is useful but omits leisure, inequality, nonmarket production, and environmental quality.
- **Ignoring expectations**: inflation, interest rates, and policy effects depend on what people expected before the policy.
- **Mixing identities with policy claims**: an identity must hold, but it does not by itself say what caused what.

## Worked Example
A collapse in consumer confidence shifts aggregate demand left, lowering output and the price level in the short run.

## Key Takeaways
1. Economic fluctuations are economy-wide and irregular.
2. Long-run output depends on real factors; short-run output can deviate from natural output.
3. Demand and supply shocks have different inflation-output signatures.

Macroeconomic policy debates turn on uncertainty, credibility, incentives, long-run costs, and how much discretion policymakers should have.

## Mental Models
- Separate **real variables** from **nominal variables** before explaining causality.
- Distinguish **accounting identities** from behavioral theories.
- Ask whether the question is about the **long run** or the **short run**; Mankiw's answer often changes with the time horizon.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating GDP as welfare**: GDP is useful but omits leisure, inequality, nonmarket production, and environmental quality.
- **Ignoring expectations**: inflation, interest rates, and policy effects depend on what people expected before the policy.
- **Mixing identities with policy claims**: an identity must hold, but it does not by itself say what caused what.

## Worked Example
A balanced-budget rule may reduce debt discipline problems but can force tax increases or spending cuts during recessions, worsening downturns.

## Key Takeaways
1. Reasonable economists can disagree because macro policy has competing risks.
2. Credibility and expectations are central to policy effects.
3. Short-run stabilization and long-run fiscal sustainability often pull in different directions.
